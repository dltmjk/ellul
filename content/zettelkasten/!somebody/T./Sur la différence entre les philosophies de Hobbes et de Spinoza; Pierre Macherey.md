For reasons which are historical and theoretical, one has, for a long time, considered the thought of Hobbes and Spinoza alike and, as a consequence,  taken to compare and confront the two. But today, it does no longer come to mind to confuse the two, nor even to place them on the same line, like if they had developed, in distinct but non exclusive forms, a doctrine similar to each other.

Above all, it was not enough to notice that the two philosophies are different, and to list all the points in which they diverge; it is necessary to reflect more on this difference itself and attempt to explain it : this is the question that G. Boss [G. Boss, la difference des philosophies, Hume at Spinoza, 1982] formulated with clarity and exhaustive examples in his work on Hume and Spinoza. A time that has shown in which the thought of Hobbes and that of Spinoza diverges on such and such point, it is evidently necessary to ask the reason, or the reasons, of this divergence, and attempting to make clear the proper philosophical significance. However, the difference which separates the forms of philosophical thought perhaps are presented in three different ways: one can say that we reason from premises so distant from each other that they can only be conducted towards conclusions no less distant, or that we from similar premises, we reason in manners so distinct that those same premises become progressively more distant from each other. Or finally, by using principles and developing argumentation that is incompatible, these premises differ in a way that we can call radical, such that sketching their relations becomes problematic. 

These three cases are not mutually exclusive from each other. In identifying these, one gives solely the means of listing the question of the difference between philosophies, and of the finding the ones most near.
