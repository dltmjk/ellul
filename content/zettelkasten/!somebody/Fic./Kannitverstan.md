First Part: Summary of the narrative 

A young man, from Kassel, and, while he couldn't remember his childhood, he knew he had lived near Winterberg and Willingen in the snow. Thin fingers, large ears, and feet that never knew where to step. As a boy his mother found him hiding in the garden with his hands clasped over his ears. She was an artist, or at least she called herself an artist even if she never believed it. 

Her copies of Vermeer, Rubens, Velázquez. 

Around the house the boy found flowers printed on vases, caricatured drawing, and soft prints of the ecce homo. She called them her 'frivolities' and never 'art'.  

Otherwise, all she had were her reproductions, which paid little but which she carried out with a mechanical intensity. For reasons the boy never understood, and for which she could never explain herself, she would keep certain finished pieces to herself. Picking them almost at random, she would store them carefully in an untouched corner of the attic where nobody would have to stare at them again. 

His father was a fisherman, short and brutish, like all men of his generation and of the generation before and to come. At the start of the boys fifteenth year they both made a pilgrimage deep into the woods. After nearly a day of walking the boy encountered a lighted bonfire with a flame reaching as high as the silver firs. In the morning he remembered only the image of a small wounded pyre. They didn't speak much after that day. In the mirror the boy, now a man, sees a face weakly reflected, not his own, but of the pyre and of his father's. 

As an adult, years later when she had passed, he found her nightmares in sketch. The last: a drowning in daylight.

Part 2: Exposition of the narrative. 

I started my life near Winterberg in what seemed like every cold December. There is no point in telling you how it started, of those first few frozen winters, or of how and why my mother and father ever decided on each other. It's all wasted breath and I was brought up never to waste anything. All I can tell you is that I was the same as every other boy, dutifully unaware that I was a child and trying, desperately, to bring adults to my eye-level. Looking back, maybe I was precocious in all the worst ways since even now I hold my self-consciousness close to my heart as if it was pride or lust. 

The world seemed overwhelming in its mediocrity, and I, by growing into the world and inheriting that same mediocrity, was never able to bring myself to terms with the whole thing. By the whole thing, I meant the dread of it all, of everyday waking up into heat and light and then shitting and walking around and chewing mushed food and gurgling sour liquids and then the nausea of conversation until sleep and until death. My mother understood the underlying tedium and took to her art as if it were the only true and good thing in the world. She never showed me love as a mother, I knew her well but in the way that I know my friends, there was nothing parental in our relationship and even now I think of her like one thinks of an old schoolfriend. When she passed I made my way up into that attic that was barred to me as a child and found her nightmares all wonderfully drawn and recorded in a small brown notebook. I saw her paintings, her imitations, placed intentionally around a solitary sculpture of the pieta which she made after I had been born. Her hiding, smuggling of her artworks, allowed her release from her confined condition. It gave her power where she could have none and she turned it into a shrine. To this day I am not sure I loved her. 

My father and I mediated our bond through varying levels of fear. I took brutishness from my father and it never left me, not until I die. My mother gave me gentleness, sweetness, but not enough and, despite her pushing, I never found somebody that could offset my fathers influence in myself. 

I saw them once, grazing the ocean with her foot but their eyes never met mine.
