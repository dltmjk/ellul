One morning as a child, when my grandfather hadn't started his drinking and my grandmother still wore her nice dresses, my mother made the mistake of giving me the ritalin reserved for my brother and, 

because she was my dutiful mother and I was a child with a petulant child's body, decided impromptu to keep me home from school. 

Being too young, unaware generally and unaware of fate specifically, I thanked chance or probability or whatever I knew it as before somebody else named it for me. 

The truth was that my mother never had the best eyesight and so it was a matter of time before she mixed her children up. Even after I forced her to the optometrist to allow her to faithfully fulfil the functions associated with a woman worried about her eyesight, she, against my peevish pestering, remained satisfied with dutifully carrying her new glasses in her old purse all while forgetting to ever put them on. Knowing this, it should have come as no surprise to find her, in another one of those mornings, ===clasping her hands over gentle tears. ===

I can't remember how the doctors dressed it up but it was a type of partial blindness. 

I felt this to be chance. Maybe if I badgered her more, maybe if I scheduled that yearly checkup in March instead of May or gotten my father to stay with his old job, that he hated, but had health insurance, and maybe if she had taken herself seriously for once instead of shrugging and smiling and saying that she would go soon, but not today. 

Maybe if I hadn't left her to find a new life for myself. Maybe if I had been a better son, and maybe if I had lived a different life.

As a girl of fourteen years, who later as a woman of thirty years, was responsible for the premature birth of my brother. Later known as Madeline, or Maddie, and sometimes when her friends shouted her name from below the mezzanine, Mad. To me she was mother, or ma. 

Such that my childhood seemed like one large and bright day. 

Well I wish I could be there back in that room with the light-ridden walls and the high ceiling, too high for us to touch, again and I would want to be walking down the street by the building by the corner of John Buford avenue like it was covered in two shadows and I was seeing her for the first time. I wanted it all to be concurrent, to be real and existing at the same time so that time became some sort of event in a dream now forgotten and my life would be some scene in a play where I came alive in some explosive final act. Yet I am sitting here with only memory that is alight all at once in a holding of bone measured 15 inches straight across, like none of it was ever real. 

It all seemed to stay still, and I came across it like hopscotch in a playground, like the world was nothing more than just chalk on the pavement being drawn and erased and drawn again. So when I see those young people, I see Michael and Joshua and them running away to start a new life not knowing what those words even mean, and I see the same wills in different people living across as if time had never been part of the picture. And I knew that they did it because they knew it was a sin and they would pay for it as if it were a sin, as if God was never part of the picture and His judgement never mattered. They held suffering in such high esteem because they chose it, they became masters over their own punishment, so when he came in and found that both He and him had left, there was nothing for Joshua to do but leave and face what had been chosen by men so different and alike years ago. 

As if it all had stayed bright and still, like a snowglobe, and we created that motion by running around without knowing even slightly what we were doing.
