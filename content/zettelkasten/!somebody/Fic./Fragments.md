One morning as a child, when my grandfather hadn't started his drinking and my grandmother still wore her nice dresses, my mother made the mistake of giving me the ritalin reserved for my brother and, because she was my dutiful mother and I was a child with a petulant child's body, decided impromptu to keep me home from school. 

Being too young, unaware generally and unaware of fate specifically, I thanked chance or probability or whatever I knew it as before somebody else named it for me. The truth was that my mother never had the best eyesight so it was a matter of time before she mixed me and my brother up. Even after I forced her to the optometrist to allow her to faithfully fulfill the functions associated with a woman worried about her eyesight, she, against my peevish pestering, remained satisfied with dutifully carrying her new glasses in her old purse all while forgetting to ever put them on. Knowing this, it should have come as no surprise to find her, in another one of those mornings, ===clasping her hands over gentle tears. ===

I can't remember how the doctors dressed it up but it was a type of partial blindness. 

I felt this to be chance. Maybe if I badgered her more, maybe if I scheduled that yearly checkup in March instead of May or gotten my father to stay with his old job, that he hated, but had health insurance, and maybe if she had taken herself seriously for once instead of shrugging and smiling and saying that she would go soon, but not today. 

Maybe if I hadn't left her to find a new life for myself. Maybe if I had been a better son, and maybe if I had lived a different life.

Such that my childhood seemed like one large and bright day.
