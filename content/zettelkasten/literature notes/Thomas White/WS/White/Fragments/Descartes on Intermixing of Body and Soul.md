The common wisdom that Desecrates instated a strict dichotomy between body and soul has been
put under some scrutiny in recent years. Jean-Luc Marion in his On Descartes’ Passive Thought,
argues that for Descartes sensing is a mode of thought exactly because the soul cannot think
without a body. Somewhat similarly, Blum, in “Substance Dualism in Descartes,” argues against
the alleged substance dualism in Descartes on the basis of the mutual dependence through the
senses. Both build upon the work of Brown, who points out that “in the Sixth Meditation … both
the mind’s real distinction from and substantial unity with the body are argued for in the same
train of thought” (Descartes and the Passionate Mind: 4–5). Still, the distinction did not lose its hold
on historians of philosophy. Gaudemard, for example, in Rethinking Descartes’s Substance Dualism,
argues that Descartes is a substance dualist based on his own definition of the term, as does Ben-
Yami in Descartes’ Philosophical Revolution. In the wider scholarly world, the relations between
Descartes and the mind-body dichotomy is unassailable. C.f. the exchange between Urban and
Brooke in the Journal of Analytical Psychology. The titles are instructive: Urban, “On matters of
Mind and Body: Regarding Descartes;” Brooke, “Descartes’ dualism and the phenomenological
tradition;” Urban, “Response to Roger Brooke’s critique.
