Tillotson referred to him and to the Pope together as being the "two great wits
[or intellectual leaders] of their church."27 He might have added that the two
were effectively rivals, for, as we shall see in Chapter 5, White led a whole
group of English Catholics, who questioned, inter alia, the Pope's infallibility.
Taking their name, 'Blackloists', from his own most-used pseudonym or alias,
they were finally outlawed, but not before White's own theological notoriety
had been officially confirmed by the condemnation and prohibition of all his
writings by the Catholic authorities in Rome. [[Covetous of Truth; Southgate]] p. 10

This contemporary notoriety was further enhanced by his one foray into
political philosophy, and it is this that largely contributed to his subsequent
historical eclipse. White's Grounds of Obedience and Government, published
under Cromwell's regime in 1655, was written to advise fellow-Catholics that
opposition to Charles I had been justified, and to recommend recognition of
Cromwell's authority as Protector. Not surprisingly, this message was widely
interpreted as being anti-royalist, and at the Restoration White was almost
totally isolated: rejected by Protestants as a typical trouble-making Catholic,
and repudiated by fellow Catholics as an unrepresentative heretic, White's
doctrines were reported from Rome to be held "in so great a detestation here
that neither that of Luther or Calvin was ever in a greater"28; and following the
blanket censorship of all his works, White's reputation was to be mainly as a
man "much ... censured at Rome."29 Nor did such Catholic disapproval ensure
his safety in England: with his ideas known to the new king personally, at the
Restoration he fled to Holland, literally in fear of his life. [[Covetous of Truth; Southgate]] p. 10
