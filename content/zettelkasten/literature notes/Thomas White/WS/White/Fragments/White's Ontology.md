Sensation as mechanical: 
Our bodies in fact contain a system of sense-organs linked with a network of
narrow channels, through which received sensations are conveyed. Appropriate
messages are carried swiftly from the very extremities of our bodies to our inner
brain: as White explains, "the least impression made in any extremity of the
body must needs, in a moment, run to their fountain."8 And that fountain, the
brain, is itself a physical entity, the material constitution of which actually
determines different mental types. So, for example, "they whose brain is of a
thin and hot constitution, easily apprehend, conjecture happily, opine rashly and
changeably: they whose disposition is more dry and thin, have a good memory
and remembrance too, but opine lightly and changeably. They that have a
temperate brain, have the best judgement."9 So the condition of our body
accounts for our psychology, and even those mental characteristics that might
affect our response to sensations are reduced to physical states. The whole
process of sensation, which had so long appeared a mystery, is thus readily
explainable in purely material terms: sights and sounds, smells and tastes, are
all corporeal, their operation ultimately reducible to mechanistic principles.

According to White, furthermore, it is not only the acquisition of knowledge
that can be thus explained, but also its subsequent recall as memory. That is,
sensations and perceptions, which are themselves mechanical operations, can,
after once being experienced, be retrieved in accordance with those same
mechanical principles: "Through all the senses, except Hearing, the Animal is
enabled by Atoms constantly sticking in it, to make use again of the Impres-
sions made by Objects." [[Covetous of Truth; Southgate]]
