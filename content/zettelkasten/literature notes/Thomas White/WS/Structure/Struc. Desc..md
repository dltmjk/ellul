Thomas White (1593–1676) and Baruch Spinoza (1632 - 1677) are both inheritors of the Cartesian tradition with some noteworthy similarities between their ideas, in particular, their brands of metaphysical monism and ideas of human freedom. This essay will attempt to explain some part of this resemblance. In the same vein as Pierre Macherey's (CITE) treatment of Hobbes and Spinoza, we will not attempt to sketch out of "the various ways in which they agree on such and such point", instead we try to explain how some features of their "thoughts arose from the same source". This source being Descartes metaphysics and philosophy of mind. 

===[1] Descartes weakens his strict dichotomy of mind and body through the introduction of his passions
[2] White and Spinoza both use this groundwork and "finish" the Cartesian project by reducing the mind into a causal process. 
[3] This leads to White's and Spinoza's relative brands of metaphysical monism===

White and Spinoza both extend upon the groundwork laid by the results and methods of Descartes in order to "finish" the Cartesian project of trying to represent the world as a unified structure of causes and effects. For both White and Spinoza, this project failed because Descartes was either unwilling or unable to apply these same commitments to his concept of mind and, as a consequence, turned man into an "imperium in imperio" (an empire within an empire). Importantly, this specific Latin phrasing is employed verbatim by both of our authors. 

Our argument begins by focusing on the transition of Descartes philosophy of mind from his earlier work, the Meditations of 1647, to the "Les Passions de l'âme", completed two years later.

Within the Meditations, Descartes defines nature as "nothing other than God himself or the ordered network of created things which was instituted by God" (Descartes, Meditations, Meditation Six, 45). This "ordered network" is not a type of hierarchical value system applied by God onto objects, but the way that objects interact causally in accordance with fixed physical laws. Descartes gives an example of how this network can lead to sensation:  

"when I see a stick, one should not think that some “intentional species” wing their way
from the stick to the eye, but merely that rays of light reflected from the stick excite certain
motions in the optic nerve"
(Descartes, Meditations, “Reply to Sixth Set of Objections,” 175.)

The above aspects of Descartes' mechanistic philosophy, and his attempts at reducing the material world into simple matter in motion, have been well recorded ([citations](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/descartes-physics/)). This is the metaphysical picture that Descartes builds his theory of mind upon, our interest is the interaction between both. 

Descartes puts forward, what we will call, a "strong" view (SM) of the mind, in which the mind is exempt from those physical laws that affect all other things. This immutable part of Descartes' metaphysics was required in order to explain error, i.e., how it is we can be deceived, or mistaken, in general. This "theory of error is based on the attribution of judgment to the faculty of (free) will” (Di Bella, “Leibniz on Error,” 713). Since the rest of reality is causally deterministic, the only "source of ... errors" is found in the will, outside of "things". 

(Descartes, Meditations, Meditation One, 4)
