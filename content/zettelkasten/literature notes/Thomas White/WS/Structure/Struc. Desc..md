Thomas White (1593–1676) and Baruch Spinoza (1632 - 1677) are both inheritors of the Cartesian tradition with some noteworthy similarities between their ideas, in particular, their brands of metaphysical monism and ideas of human freedom. This essay will attempt to explain some part of this resemblance. In the same vein as Pierre Macherey's (CITE) treatment of Hobbes and Spinoza, we will not sketch out "the various ways in which they agree on such and such point", instead we will try to explain how some features of their "thoughts arose from the same source". This source being Descartes' metaphysics and philosophy of mind.

===[1] Descartes weakens his strict dichotomy of mind and body through the introduction of his passions
[2] White and Spinoza both use this groundwork and "finish" the Cartesian project by reducing the mind into a causal process. 
[3] This leads to White's and Spinoza's relative brands of metaphysical monism===

White and Spinoza both extend upon the groundwork laid by the results and methods of Descartes in order to fully extend the Cartesian project of representing the world as a unified structure of causes and effects. For both White and Spinoza, this project failed because Descartes was either unwilling, or unable, to apply those same commitments to his concept of mind and, as a consequence, turned man into an "imperium in imperio" (an empire within an empire). It's important to note that this specific Latin phrasing is employed verbatim by both of our authors. Our argument begins by focusing on the transition of Descartes philosophy of mind from his earlier work, the Meditations of 1641, to the "Les Passions de l'âme", completed seven years later.

Later Descartes weakens both the responsibilities of the mind and the distinction between mind and body, in doing so he creates a new 'union' of the 'passions'. This was not a sudden change, even within his earlier Discours de la Method (year) he maintained that the mind "must be more closely joined and united with the body" (Descartes, Discourse on Method, CSM 1, 141). The Principles (cite) develops this "joining" further by arguing that hunger, thirst, sadness, anger, hardness, light and colour (the passions) all "originate from the close and profound union of our mind with the body" (CITE). His *Passions of the Soul*, the last word on the subject, describes the reciprocal nature of this 'profound union' (CITE). 

> the movements which are thus excited in the brain by the nerves affect the soul or mind [...] and these diverse states or thoughts of the mind, following immediately from these movements, are called the perceptions of the senses (Descartes, Principles, art. 189, 276.)

Interactions within this union are not asymmetrical (as they were previously). They are mutually affecting as "the soul has to move the body and the body to act on the soul, in causing sensations and passions" (l'ame & le corps ensemble – AT 3:665). At another point, Descartes asserts that "no subject that acts more immediately upon our soul than the body it is joined to" (375). In SM the role of the mind is to "direct the whole body [within ]its principal seat in the brain; there alone it not only understands and imagines" (Descartes, Principles, art. 189, 276). Whereas in the passions, the mind can be acted upon in a mechanical manner by the body. 

We can call this conception of Descartes' mind the "weak" view (WM). The most marked shift from SM to WM is applying the language of movement to the mind, for Descartes "perceptions or sensations or excitations of the soul [...] are caused, maintained, and strengthened by some movement of the spirits (Descartes, Passions I, Art. 27-8, 33-4.)". The mind still had a set of required functional responsibilities, however by developing its affectibility to material components, we can view it as a material (and therefore causal) thing. These functional responsibilities, furthermore, were also reallocated:

> the principal effect of all the passions in men is that they incite and dispose their soul to will the things for which they prepare their body, so that the sensation of fear incites it to will to flee, that of boldness to will to do battle, and so on for the rest. Descartes, Passions I, Art. 40, 40-1.

Within the Passions, the immateriality of the mind can appear as a relic of previous Cartesian system, and if viewed unfavourably, as an active contradiction. One could argue that the slow acquiescing of causal logic to the mind is the natural completion of his previous metaphysical commitments and method (as recorded in the first eight rules of the regulae). Whether or not Descartes would've actually taken this to it's ultimate conclusion, and fully mechanised the mind, is unknown. The more radical inheritors of the Cartesian tradition, of which I count both White and Spinoza, have this unsolved relationship between mind and reality as their starting point. 

However, removing it completely hinges upon if its standard responsibilities can be completely re-allocated to the body.

White argues that the Descartes maintained the necessity of the soul because the body alone cannot make out "Quantities, Distances, Figures and Colours of things.”. Therefore one had to assume a new entity, which in this case is non-physical, in order to properly comprehend these properties. White instead argues that they were mistaken in thinking that “the Soul in the Body [is] a kind of thing, not the form or affection of the thing, Man”

(White, An Exclusion of Scepticks, 35.)

This is a very Aristotelian view, which White admits plainly. A noticeable feature of his thought is the lack of any doctrinal purity a fact which led to Hobbes remarking that he was "torn between two obligations" (Jones ed., Hobbes, p. 162. Southgate Book). 

White goes on to argue that the mechanism of the mind is entirely mechanical “The first stroak produces Knowledge; the later, actuall Memory.” (CITE 155). His reasoning is that if man and nature are both part of the world, they are liable to the same laws, and are both matter-in-motion. White's style of argument can be traced directly to Descartes when he claims that "everything in us which we conceive entirely incapable of belonging to a body must be attributed to our soul" (390). If we can attribute the functions of the mind entirely to a material body, there is no need for the mind or the immaterial realm it exists within. However, White's position is not cut and dry as this, that is, he does not go as far as making the mind completely redundant.  The mind still "acquires knowledge from the Body … Yet, [it] has not, by this Science, a power to move the body" (White, Institutions, Bk. IV, Le. X. 3-5, 236-7). 

Spinoza has a similar approach, writing in his Tractatus de Intellectus Emendatione that the ancients believed "that true science proceeds from cause to effect, except that, as far as I know, they never conceived the soul, as we are here doing, as acting according to fixed laws". His point being that the soul, or the mind, is also liable to fixed physical laws usually applied to nature. We find this corroborated in the Ethics: 

"when men say that this or that action of the body arises from the mind which has command over the
body, they do not know what they are saying, and are merely admitting … that they are ignorant of the
true cause of that action and are not concerned to discover it"
