## Epistemic Consequentialism 
1. Subjective and Objective consequentialism  
	1. Subjective - what one ought to do is *think* in terms of promoting the good. An agent should be *trying* to achieve the epistemically good and this *trying* is what matters.
		1. Subjective consequentialists hold that "promoting the good" is always a function that is applied to an agents beliefs and desires. The corresponding action, whether it not it succeeds at promoting the good, is right relative to the agents psychology. 
			1. Another way of putting this is that the *right* action is one that maximizes expected utility given a unique agents beliefs and desires. 
		2. Multiple constraints are added onto this to preserve its stability. *Reasonableness* is applied. 
			1. Frank Jackson provides a decision theoretic version. 
				1. What an agent *ought to do* is a combination of the agents probability function (what the agents believes) and what the agent ought to desire. 
		3. The use of the subjective standard is to answer two questions 
			1. Moral luck problems: when something goes wrong despite the correct intentions. Therefore, rightness should be attributed to a correct intention
			2. Agent-ownership: Judgements of rightness are made right before an agent commits a certain action.  
				1. There has to be some sort of connection between the action and the agents psychology in order to classify said action as an *action of the agent*. 
	2. Objective - The rightness of an act is the fact of whether it would promote the good of acts available to the agent. The acts that would bring about the best outcomes. What matters if it is actually epistemically good or not.
		1. Best outcomes, without any commitment to a strand of decision making. 
	3. Generally they seem to offer competing standards of right.
2. The best approach might actually be to not invest in one type of decision making in *trying* but instead cultivate certain habits and dispositions that naturally bring about the epistemically good. 
	1. The standard set by objective consequentialism is as such:
		> (OEC) The right belief is the belief that promotes the epistemic good
	2. The question of whether one should *try* and promote the epistemic good is an "empirical issue"
		1. What does this mean?
	3. One could produce certain habits that lead to truth, not the same as process reliabilism, since habits are evaluated based on their conduciveness to an epistemic good and not on whether they are reliable processes.
