This paper critically examines the dominant Simulation Theory (ST) of social cognition, identifying its fundamental Starting and Diversity problems. It then evaluates the Narrative Practice Hypothesis (NPH) as a proposed alternative. The analysis addresses two major counterarguments against the NPH: an internalisation challenge, which claims narrative understanding requires prior perspective-taking, and a diversity challenge, which argues narratives domesticate otherness. In rebuttal, this paper reframes internalisation not as perspective-taking but as the creative recombination of narrative structures. It defends this thesis by invoking the phenomenon of children’s imaginary companions, arguing that this practice exemplifies narrative internalisation while simultaneously fostering theory-of-mind capabilities. The paper concludes that a properly formulated narrative account, supported by developmental evidence, offers a robust framework for understanding social cognition that avoids the pitfalls of simulationist approaches.

This paper examines the role of narrative practices in empathy and social cognition. Two major counterarguments are addressed: Bailey's internalisation counterargument, which claims narrative accounts covertly depend on perspective-taking, and Zahavi's diversity counterargument, which suggests narratives domesticate otherness by reducing agents to familiar story patterns. In rebuttal, this paper reframes internalisation not as perspective-taking but as the recombination of narrative structures and events. We defend this account by examining the phenomenon of imaginary companions (ICs) in children, arguing that IC creation demonstrates narrative internalisation at work. 


In response, we propose a different formulation of narrative internalisation as the ability to recombine and apply narrative structures rather than to simply memorise or inhabit them. We defend this account by examining the phenomenon of imaginary companions (ICs) in children, arguing that IC creation demonstrates narrative internalisation at work. Empirical evidence linking ICs to theory-of-mind development and the structured, causally-ordered nature of IC interactions supports the claim that narrative competence precedes and enables perspective-taking rather than depending upon it.

\subsection{Preliminaries}

Much work in the philosophy of empathy and social cognition depends upon the result of mirror neurons (MNs). These show that our own motor processes replicate the observed behaviour of another agent, vindicating Hume's claim that “The minds of men are mirrors to one another” (1739/1978: 365). Debates over the implications of this result have split philosophers into two camps. Vittorio Gallese (2001; 2003), part of the first, and of the original team that discovered MNs, argues that a "neural matching mechanism" is largely responsible for our phenomenal experience of empathy (Gallese 2001, 44). I.e., according to Gallese, empathy is nothing more than our ability to ascribe mental states to others. 

This strong reading of Gallese equates empathy with the process of 'mindreading', which refers to an agent’s ability to identify the mental states (e.g., beliefs, desires, sensations) of another (CITE). By making this connection, he delineates three different levels of cognition:

1. A Subpersonal Level: This is "instantiated as the level of activity of a series of mirror matching neural circuits" 
2. A Functional Level: Characterised "in terms of ‘as if modes’ of interaction enabling models of self-other to be created".  
3. A Phenomenological level: Responsible for "the sense of similarity, of being individuals within a larger social community of persons like us, which we experience any time we confront ourselves with other human beings" ([Gallese 2003](https://sci-hub.se/10.1159/000072786), p. 177 for all).

Therefore, empathy is reduced to these motor 'resonance processes', since when we observe the actions of another agent "our motor system 'resonates’ along with that of the observed agent" (Gallese 2001: 38). The second camp argues that empathy requires "something more". A major representative of this approach, John Decety (2003, 2005), challenges Gallese's Functional Level. While he maintains that empathy requires basic resonance systems, he instead proposes that insight into the perspective of another agent has to be controlled and intentional. In other words, directed in a way that makes it qualitatively richer than a simple resonance process.

Despite these differences, mutual dependence on resonance processes leads into the same Theory of Mind (ToM). Specifically, a simulationist theory (ST), which asserts that we use our own minds as a model to simulate another's intentional states (Goldman 2006). Stueber (2012) outlines three general phases: 

1. Matching phase: This requires an agent to adopt the perspective of another (consisting of beliefs and desires) and ignore their own non-relevant beliefs. 
2. Simulation phase: An agent entertains possible actions given this alternative perspective.
3. Attribution phase: An agent stops entertaining this alternative perspective and bases their perspective on the results of the simulation phase. 

Alternatively, Goldman (2005) describes it in the following way: 

> The core idea of ST is that the attributor selects a mental state for attribution after reproducing or “enacting” within herself the very state in question, or a relevantly similar state. In other words, she tries to replicate a target’s mental state by undergoing (what she takes to be) the same or a similar mental process to one the target undergoes (p. 198)

Therefore, empathy is the outcome of us mentally simulating a target's emotions, and through the simulation phase, feeling this emotion ourselves. Goldman distinguishes the two by placing Gallese's approach as a low-level (implicit) mindreading, and Decety's as a high-level (explicit) mindreading. According to Goldman (2006), the former is “simple, primitive, automatic, and largely below the level of consciousness (Goldman 2006, pg. 147). Therefore, the main break between Gallese and Decety is whether these phases are constrained to a subconscious functioning or not. Despite this difference, Gallagher (2012) identifies two issues that concern both higher and lower level interpretations: the starting problem and the diversity problem

\subsubsection{The Starting and Diversity Problem}
The starting problem, similar to the frame problem within cognitive science, concerns the matching phase of ST. Specifically, how we determine what is relevant given a certain situation. The challenge can be put as follows \footnote{A similar argument can be mounted against Theory-Theory ToM accounts}: 

> (SP) 1: ST claims that understanding others’ mental states begins when an observer "create in herself pretend states intended to match those of the target." (Goldman 2005b, 80–81). 
> 
> (SP) 2: For this to work, the agent must already know which pretend states (beliefs, desires, emotions) correspond to the target’s actual mental states.
> 
> (SP) 3: However, knowing which states to simulate presupposes the very capacity ST is meant to explain. Specifically, the capacity to understand what another person is thinking or feeling.
> 
> C1. Therefore, ST faces a starting problem: it cannot explain how the agent identifies the correct target state without already possessing some understanding of the other.

In response to this, advocates of ST have claimed that SP1 is informed, and guided, by our own prior first-person experiences. This kind of initial projection enables us to circumvent the problem posed by SP2, since our previous experiences, and knowledge of what we would do, provide context into another agent’s state. Following from this, SP1 is then a process in which an agent’s observation of others’ actions or emotions “is therefore transformed into an inner representation of what we would do or feel in a similar situation” (Keysers & Gazzola, p. 390). If we accept this, it seems we can escape the contradiction of SP2 and SP3 and bypass the starting problem altogether. 

In reply, Gallagher (2012) advances the diversity problem. The argument takes the following form:

> (DP) 1: ST claims that we understand others by using our own first-person experiences to simulate their mental states.
> 
> (DP) 2: In order to simulate another’s mental state, we must project from our own prior experiences \footnote{There are some empirical findings that support this. MNs appear to fire only when an agent has previously performed an action similar to the one they are now observing ([Calvo-Merino et al. 2006](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17027486/))}.
> 
> (DP) 3: Projection based on one’s own experiences means that simulated understanding is grounded in the simulator’s (i.e., the observer’s) perspective, not the other’s.
> 
> (DP) 4: Therefore, simulation risks reiterating the simulator’s own perspective rather than genuinely adopting the perspective of the other (introducing a first-person bias).
> 
> (DP) 5: Moreover, because individual experience is limited and cannot capture the vast diversity of other people’s experiences, simulation based on one’s own experience will often be inaccurate or incomplete.
> 
> Conclusion: Hence, Simulation Theory faces two problems:
	(a) it introduces a systematic first-person bias into our understanding of others, and
	(b) it fails to accommodate the diversity of others’ experiences due to the limits of one’s own experiential base.

This seems to be a very strong argument against ST. In response, advocates of ST might bite the bullet and answer “yes” to the diversity problem. The question then becomes: how are we able to empathize with people from wildly different backgrounds? Not just people, but also with anything from animals to aliens (for example, when portrayed in text or film). In essence, both the diversity and starting problems attack how strongly ST depends on the first-person position, which only concerns itself with the abilities of the simulating agent. The consequence of this is that alternative perspectives are transformed to reflect only the attributing agent’s experiences. As de Vignemont (CITE) and Jacob (CITE) both argue, empathy has to be other-directed; i.e., it cannot allow for this reduction and requires an agent to be able to grasp a variety of perspectives.

\section{Social Cognition relevance to Empathy}
As a quick aside, it is important to establish the relevance of social cognition theories to a concept of empathy. In particular, why differentiating between a simulation and narrative account is useful. To start, we need to clarify our usage and stance on what ‘empathy’ is. The following conditions for empathy are provided by de Vignemont, Singer, and Jacob (citations in Gallagher 2012)\footnote{The first edition of the list is given in de Vignemont and Singer. Later, de Vignemont and Jacob (CITE) added the caring condition in Jacob 2011.}

1. An Affectivity Condition: The “two individuals,” empathizer and target, “are experiencing some affective state”; only then can “one be said to empathize with the other” (de Vignemont and Jacob 2012, p. 304).
2. An Inter-Personal Similarity Condition: The empathizer’s affective state “stands in some relevant similarity relation to her target’s affective state” (p. 305). 
3. An Ascription Condition: The empathizer must be aware of the source of his own affective state.
4. A Caring Condition: The empathizer must care about the target's affective state and affective life. 

The theory of social cognition we apply will have different ways of meeting this criterion. For example, ST would meet the affectivity condition by appealing to the dynamics of its simulation phase. Conversely, a narrative account would claim that empathy is spurred by exposure to the target’s ‘story’. For instance, if we witness a person sitting on a park bench, we generally would not feel any empathy. Upon learning that their mother has passed away (and because we have encountered similar events before, either through experience or story) only then do we begin to empathize.

\section{Narrative Accounts}

This section will first outline what a narrative consists of. From there, we will explore how...

\subsection{What is a Narrative?}
In order to substantiate a narrative account, we need to specify exactly what separates “a sequence of events” from “a narrative or story of the events” (Goldie 2012, p. 7)\footnote{We are concerned with the normative, not ontological, nature of a narrative.}. For example, is the sentence “There was a boy who lived in a treehouse” a narrative or a mere state of affairs? We lean toward designating it as the latter. However, if we append this statement with further detail. Let's say that the boy went to school and ate a jam sandwich. Then we seem to gradually transition into something resembling a narrative. In attempting to answer this, Carroll (2010) defines a narrative as a “sequence of events” that fulfils (in the form of a gradient) the following criteria:

(NC) 1: At least two, though possibly more, events or states of affairs
(NC) 2: It contains at least one unified subject
(NC) 3: The events in the narrative are temporally ordered
(NC) 4: The events in the narrative are causally related

NC1 is what turns our original statement about the treehouse boy into an actual narrative. This is further qualified by NC2, which prevents a disparate collection of statements (e.g., “John shines shoes, Stella reads books, Anica is a doctor”) from qualifying as a narrative. NC3 and NC4 together prevent a laundry list of events and facts about a singular subject (e.g., “David is a plumber. David is 45. David went to the store”) from becoming a narrative. The events need to be causally ordered, either backwards or forwards. Therefore, to qualify as a narrative, a formulated sequence of events must meet each of these conditions, albeit at varying levels (Fabry 2021).

This is congruent with Lamarque's definition of a narrative, in which "at least two events must be depicted [...] and there must be some more or less loose, albeit non-logical, relation between the events. Crucially, there is a temporal dimension in narrative” (Lamarque 2004, p. 394).

Understandably, some might argue that this is an especially lax formulation. Velleman (2003), in particular, argues that a narrative necessitates a particular emotional response from an agent. He gives the example of two events that are not causally connected but have a karmic significance. For instance, a murder that is followed by the diagnosis of an illness in the murderer. Therefore, we update NC4 to read:

(NC4*) Events in the narrative are causally or emotionally related.

To elaborate any further would be beyond the scope of this paper. For our current purposes, the above definition will suffice.

\subsection{What is the Narrative Account?}

While our presentation will largely follow Gallagher and Hutto's (2008) Narrative Practice Hypothesis (NPH), it is important to note that both Wilhelm Schapp and Peter Goldie independently arrived at similar conclusions.\footnote{This diversity makes sense when we consider that narrative accounts of social understanding were originally formulated within the anthropology of cultural folklore (Bascom 1954).} Schapp presents "human life [as] a life that is caught up in stories; it is nothing apart from these stories, and such stories provide the only possible access to oneself and to others" (Zahavi, Self and Other, p. 188). Similarly, Goldie (2000) characterizes human understanding as an effort to locate the relevant 'episodes' of thought and feeling that correspond to, or make most sense of, an agent's emotional experience (Goldie 2000).

The NPH runs along similar lines. Gallagher and Hutto give the following explanation:

> Typically, [children] are provided with running commentaries on stories that teach them not only which actions are suited to particular situations but also which reasons for acting are acceptable and which are not. It is by absorbing such standards that we first learn how to judge an action’s appropriateness (though, of course, in time such standards are sometimes questioned and overturned). Quite generally, stories – real or fictional – teach us what others can expect from us, but just as importantly, what we can expect from others in certain situations. This is not just coming to know what others ought to (and thus are likely to) do, but what they ought to (and thus are likely to) think and feel, as indexed to the sort of people they are. (2008): 31 \footnote{Quote found in Bailey 2018}

The following provides a minimal account of a narrative account of social cognition, i.e., the very general steps it requires:

(NA) 1: A child acquires narrative competency through exposure to narrative practices such as storytelling, pretend play, and conversation.

(NA) 2: Narrative competency is therefore the normal developmental route through which agents obtain folk-psychological understanding.

(NA) 3: By internalising (or absorbing) these narratives, a child is able to "make sense" of an agent's beliefs, desires, and the reasons that underlie them (Hutto 2012).

Some, such as Stueber (2008), argue that the importance of a narrative is to inform the simulationist account. In his view, the narrative account serves an auxiliary role by providing "hints and clues" that can inform the main simulation process. Conversely, Gallagher and Hutto make the much stronger claim that narrative practices allow us to sidestep simulationist accounts entirely. They argue that NA1 reduces dependence on a perspective-taking or ST account. Generally, narratives provide an agent with a variety of contexts. Bruner and Kalmar (1998) puts it as, "a massive hermeneutical background" that allows for the understanding of various mental states. NPH seems to avoid our previous troubles with the starting and diversity problem. To elaborate, it does not have to commit to DP1, DP2, or SP1 because an agent can use familiar narratives to guide their empathising rather than being limited to a first-person perspective.

\section{Counterarguments}
This section will outline two major arguments against the NPH. We will call the first the 'internalisation counterargument', and the second the 'diversity counterargument'. 

\subsection{The Internalisation Counterargument}
Olivia Bailey (2018) challenges NA3, specifically the process of 'internalisation'. Bailey finds that the above usage of internalisation is underdeveloped. Gallagher and Hutto claim that narratives need to be "suitably internalised". Instead of providing a rigorous definition, Gallagher and Hutto substantiate their account by appeals to "skills" and "know how". On top of this, they are insistent that an autistic person would be unable to adequately internalise narratives, claiming that they do not 'internalise' the rules but only memorise them. Therefore, this understanding cannot appeal to rote memorisation \footnote{Furthermore, if rote memorisation of rules is possible, then the narrativist account collapses into the "theory-theory" model of social cognition. This is a ToM that grounds mindreading as being the usage and knowledge of certain 'theories' that guide human behaviour (Gopnik and Meltzoff 1997).}. Instead, proper internalisation requires an agent to 'absorb' a narrative.

Bailey asks what the qualitative difference between 'absorbing' a narrative and memorising one is. It has to be something that an autistic person is unable to do. Given this latter consideration, she provides the following formulation of narrative internalisation:

(I)1: Internalisation is the ability for an agent to "actually imaginatively see things in the way that narratives suggest" (Bailey, pg. 143).
(I)2: Therefore, we need to be able to imagine relevant parts of the narrative from the point of view of the character.
(I)3: Furthermore, you must be able to identify the imaginative object and point of view with the correct emotion. For example, understanding why something is scary even if you do not necessarily find it scary yourself.

I1 is necessary because it allows an agent to actually get how the events of a narrative fit together. If we return to our previous discussion of narrative composition in section X, we can see that this condition is congruent with NC4$*$ which allows for events to be emotionally connected. Following from this, Bailey argues that I2 mandates a level of imaginative capability, an ability that autistic people find harder to accomplish, matching the previous stipulation that autistic people cannot partake in internalisation. While Bailey's usage of this stipulation is partly a rhetorical trick, it works by attacking the poorly developed nature of Gallagher and Hutto's internalisation. Furthermore, she claims that "there is a difference between narratives’ being familiar and their actually making sense". This is the motivation for I3. An agent can be exposed to a variety of narratives but still be unable to make sense of them. In essence, there must be something more required to make sense of these stories.

Bailey's overall goal is to return to a first-person, or perspective-taking account, of social cognition. Her formulation of I2 and I3 characterises this something more as perspective taking, showing that a narrative account requires one. You cannot grasp the meaning of a narrative if you do not understand what the events signify for the subjects. Therefore, you must engage in perspective taking before being able to engage with narratives. Before engaging with Bailey's account, we will detail another argument against the NPH that attacks on similar grounds.

\subsection{The Diversity Counterargument}
Dan Zahavi (2015) objects to NA2 and NA3. He claims that they entail "what could be called a domestication of otherness: you reduce the other to that which can be captured in narratives" (CITE pg. 189). This criticism is prompted by Schapp's claim that "to know or meet somebody in person is merely to encounter new stories or have the old stories confirmed" \footnote{Quote taken from Zahavi 2015; Schapp 2004: 105–6}.

We can refashion this as a type of diversity problem for the narrative account.

(ZD)1: Narratives provide a way to interpret the behaviour of others.
(ZD)2: Internalisation consists in applying this narrative store to the actions of people.
(ZD)3: Therefore, all behaviour must be interpreted in the form of this past narrative experience.
(ZD)4: However, people are characterised by a certain otherness which does not neatly fit into narratives.

From this, we can say:

(ZC)1: Either the narrative account has to accept that we can only view people in relation to previously exposed narratives.

The analogy with our previous diversity problem for ST is apparent. Instead of being constrained by the first-person perspective, the narrative account needs to explain how people can exceed the expectations of the narratives we place on them. In response, Zahavi also proposes an alternative account of social cognition, where it is our shared engagement with the modern world that acts as the bedrock for understanding. He continues by arguing that "the very attempt to grasp thematically the experiences of others is the exception rather than the rule" (pg. 191). Intersubjective understanding, then, is not accrued through narrative competence, but by being socially and culturally embedded. Given this, we can also put forward:

(ZC)2: The narrative account actually depends on an alternative form of understanding provided by social and cultural embeddedness.

\subsection{Rebuttal}
Both Zahavi and Bailey's points concern what internalisation is and how it proceeds \footnote{In the case of Zahavi, this is ZD2}. Either it requires perspective-taking, or it is too limited to act as an actual mechanism of social cognition. In both cases, it must collapse into some other theory. In responding to this, we need to go over the hidden assumptions in both arguments. First, Zahavi makes the implicit claim, in ZD2 and ZD3, that there is no possible way to extend beyond a rote-internalisation. That is, that the mechanism can only proceed through reduction into a narrative and internalisation can only be this mechanism. On the other hand, Bailey assumes that internalisation can only mean placing oneself in the narrative, instead of some other process that assists in the narrative account.

Given this, it seems that a different account of internalisation that escapes ZD2 would be able to avoid Zahavi's diversity problem. Hopefully, this richer account will also be able to explain the process of internalisation without having to depend on any perspective-taking, contra Bailey. This section will provide an alternative characterisation of internalisation which responds to both counterarguments. In attempting this, we will use the phenomenon of imaginary companions (IC) in children as an example. Our formulation depends on two claims:

(In) 1: Internalisation is actually the ability to mix events, parts, and sections of narratives we have been exposed to.
(In) 2: We perform, or demonstrate, this ability to internalise through a number of different activities.

\subsubsection{Defending In1}
Before we can substantiate In1, we need to return to how Gallagher and Hutto support NA1 and NA2 in their original NPH. In particular, how their utilisation of primary and secondary intersubjectivity.

Primary intersubjectivity concerns the behaviour of an infant soon after birth and their ability to imitate the gestures and expressions of others (Meltzoff & Moore, 1977, 1989). This phenomenon takes place when perception and action are combined at the same sensory and motor-function levels (Trevarthen, 1979). According to Stern (1998), these imitative movements evoke corresponding feelings in the infant when in dynamic action with their caretaker. Therefore, perception and action are combined and performed without any type of simulation.

Secondary intersubjectivity is the continuation of this process. The infant goes beyond their imitative capability to enter into contexts and situations where they are able to grasp what things mean by seeing what they are for. Gallagher and Hutto put it as such:

> The child can understand that the other person wants food or intends to open the door; that the other can see him (the child) or is looking at the door. This is not taking an intentional stance, i.e., treating the other as if they had desires or beliefs hidden away in their minds; rather, the intentionality is perceived in the embodied actions of others (Gall & Hutto Primary Interaction). 

Continuing from this, they assert that when the capacities that are linked to primary and secondary intersubjectivity are combined, the ability for narrative competence develops, specifying that “children normally achieve [folk-psychological] understanding by engaging in storytelling practices, with the support of others... Stories of this special kind provide the crucial training set needed for understanding reasons.” (Hutto 2007b: 53).

In essence, understanding narrative reasons requires the agent to abstract certain reasons and construct their own narratives. Therefore, it is best to conceive of internalisation as the re-composition and application of these narrative structures and motifs. They give the following example: “Children’s first narrative productions occur in action, in episodes of symbolic play by groups of peers [...] Play is an important developmental source of narrative” (p. 11, citing Nelson 2003). In this case, action and symbolic play are the extensions of these narratives. It is these activities that we will consider characteristic of In2.

The question becomes: what are the reasons for accepting our version of internalisation over Bailey's?
In order to bolster our own account, we can locate some inconsistencies in her account. Specifically, we note how the connection between I2 and I3 in Bailey's account seems incorrect. For example, we can understand desires that we cannot bring ourselves to imagine. Desires that are horrifying, but that we can understand at a general level. In detailing her perspective-taking account, she gives the example of the giants from Jack and the Beanstalk crushing up children. She claims:

> I grant that most of us can’t see bone-grinding in particular as a good thing, but we can see taking steps to alleviate hunger as desirable (CITE)

However, she claims that we understand this latter desire through our imaginative perspective taking. If this were the case, then she has to provide a reason for why we are able to correlate our desires with theirs. In other words, what are the reasons we have for believing that this giant works similarly to us? This warrant is not given to us by any application of our own perspective, but by exposure to the rest of the narrative. For example, the narrative establishes that the giants have desires similar to humans, which is what allows us to understand that they have this desire to alleviate hunger. It is not our perspective superimposed on theirs that grants this knowledge, but the corresponding parts of the narrative.

Therefore, the internalisation counterargument gets the order mixed up. We do not imagine parts of the narrative from the point of view of a character but learn about possible actions a character can take through the narrative. This latter mechanism does not require the agent to take on any sort of perspective-based imaginative work. Furthermore, as shown in Richner & Nicolopoulou (2001), children learn of these possible actions and soon after integrate these actions into their own perspectives by mimicking them. In essence, the possibility of action is provided and then employed.

This seems to disarm the challenge since B2 no longer requires any perspective taking. In fact, B2 now fails because narrative accounts and internalisation must precede all talk of perspective taking. The logic is that it is hard to talk about taking another perspective if you do not know what perspectives are available. For us to be able to imagine other points of view, we must be able to go beyond our first-person view. This ability is given to us by the internalisation of certain narratives.

Still, we have to detail what this 're-composition' and 'application' of narrative internalisation means. To show this, we invoke the phenomenon of Imaginary Companions (ICs) in children. Furthermore, we use ICs to dispel Zahavi's diversity argument.

\subsubsection{Claim 2}
In Gopnik's book The Philosophical Baby, she explores the construction and use of ICs in children. As we see, there seems to be a link between the functional use of fiction and of ICs. Gopnik (CITE) points out: "Children who watched a lot of television were less likely to have ICs, but that was also true of children who read a lot of books" (p. 52). There seems to be an intuitive reason for connecting the narratives provided by fiction and the employment of ICs. There is no foolproof way of connecting the phenomenon of ICs and a narrative account. However, we can provide some convincing reasons for making this leap.

First, let's see how ICs connect with developing a ToM. Taylor and Carlson (1997) found that 3–4-year-olds who engaged in fantasy play (including imaginary friends) scored higher on theory-of-mind tasks. In particular, research finds significantly fewer children with autism invent imaginary friends than typical peers (e.g., ~16% vs. 42% in one study [CITE](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29564680/#:~:text=with%20ASD%29%20gender,ASD%20conceptualize%20their%20ICs%27%20minds)). Crucially, autistic children who do create ICs gain similar social-cognitive benefits. Davis et al. (2023) report that autistic kids with imaginary companions showed better theory-of-mind performance and stronger social skills than autistic children without ICs.

It seems we have good empirical reason for believing that there is a connection between ICs and a developed ToM. However, what reason do we have to believe that ICs are connected with, or support, a narrative account? Given that we develop our capacities for narrative thinking, imaginary companions, and empathy at around the same period, we seem to have *some* reason for believing that they are inter-related (Decety and Jackson, 2004; Bretherton, Fritz, Zahn-Waxler, & Ridgeway, 1986). If we look at other developmental literature on ICs, we can see that they fulfil our requirements for a narrative.

(NC1) seems to be backed up by Taylor (1999) who reports that most ICs participate in repeated joint activities. For example, helping the child clean their room or joining in meals. Each interaction constitutes a discrete “event” within an ongoing imaginative storyline. Furthermore, Taylor and Carlson (1997) found that children consistently describe their ICs as having distinct personalities and motives that evolve over time, which seems to fulfil (NC2) and (NC3). (NC4) is substantiated by Taylor (1999) and Davis et al. (2014) who both note that the child attributes emotions and motivations to the companion (“she was sad because I shouted at her”) and constructs causally linked storylines that explain the companion’s actions based on these emotional reactions.

This storyline element is the most important, specifically because it shows that children ground these imaginative experiences within a narrative framework. It seems that these activities of make-believe play and ICs both point to the same process which assists in building a coherent ToM. Our argument is that this is narrative internalisation at work. Still, the challenge can be made that all of these elements are circumstantial and that these underlying processes are something else entirely. However, the above elements give us some reason to accept a narrative account.

If we accept this connection, then Zahavi's ZD2 is incorrect because internalisation, as shown in ICs and make-believe, is the extending of these narrative accounts onto novel situations. Even if we reject this connection, our alternative internalisation formulation still gives us good reason to reject ZD2.
