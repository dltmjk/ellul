An important distinction within the structure of empathy is often made between emotional/affective and cognitive aspects. Affective empathy is commonly understood as an affective state (such as the experience of emotion, pain or reward), caused by sharing the state of another person through observation or imagination of their experience [[1]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0005), [[5]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0025). Although an observer’s emotional state is isomorphic with that of another person, the observer is aware that someone else is the source of that state [[5]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0025). Cognitive aspects of empathy are commonly referred to as perspective taking, mentalising or theory of mind. Combined, these processes enable an observer to understand another person’s beliefs, desires and emotions [[13]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0065). In this review, both components are seen as important contributors to the experience of empathy (in line with [[9]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0045)). However, it is important to note that some authors define empathy as comprised only of the “affective” components and label the “cognitive” components as a separate but related construct of “theory of mind” or “mentalising” on the basis that they rely on largely distinct neurocognitive circuits (e.g. [[14]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4942880/#bib0070)).
