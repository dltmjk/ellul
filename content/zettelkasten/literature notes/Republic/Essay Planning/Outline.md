"So when currency had been now invented as an outcome of the necessary interchange of goods, there came into existence the other form of wealth-getting, trade, which at first no doubt went on in a simple form, but later became more highly organized as experience discovered the sources and methods of exchange that would cause most profit" Aristot. 3. Disruption of the Aristocracy
	1. The aristocracy held an attitude which praised gift exchange (as it was performed in archaic Greece, when the aristocrats still held power over most means of exchange) and castigated commodity exchange. 
		1. This was out of a aversion to acknowledging the agora, which utilizes coinage, as an alternate civic authority (coins, bodies games)
	2. This sort of attitude towards trade, and therefore coinage, was found within the associations of the *kapeleia* (Kurke)
		1. *kapeleia* (retail trade) is in contrast to *emporoi* (long distance trade)
			1. The difference is summed up in Rep. 371d
				1. "Or is not shopkeepers the name (kapeleia) we give to those who, planted in the agora, serve us in buying and selling, while we call those who roam from city to city merchants (emporoi)"Rep. 371d
			2. While aristocrats practised trade, this did not constitute the entirety of their social identity. Whereas the *kapeleia* was a professional obligation. For the aristocrat, the *kapelos* produces nothing and functions only be exercising deceit. 
		2. Aristocratic attitudes towards the *kapeleia* were mostly negative and indicated a type of deceit. 
			1. Anacharsis "defined the agora as a place for deceiving each other and claiming more than one's share" Diogenes Laertius 1.105
			2. Passages from Herodotus mirror this sentiment 
				1. Kroisos advice to Kyros on how to educate a child in order to make them weak 
					1. "proclaim to them that they train their sons to play the lyre and the harp and to be retail-dealers; and soon thou shalt see, O king, that they have become women instead of men" Herodotus 1.155 
			3. Those who made their money through such trading activities were labeled "kakoi" (base-born) by the aristocratic class. A pejorative term used to insult. 
		3. Passages within the Republic mirror this aristocratic scorn. 
			1. "If, then, the farmer or any other craftsman taking his products to the market-place does not arrive at the same time with those who desire to exchange with him, is he to sit idle in the market-place and lose time from his own work?” “By no means,” he said, “but there are men who see this need and appoint themselves for this service—in well-conducted cities they are generally those who are weakest in body and those who are useless for any other task. 	3. This is because the class interests of the aristocrats (maintaining control over land and labour) differs greatly from those of the commercial class (wealth in good and power to buy labour). The commercial class disrupted the most important parts of the aristocrats class continued influence. Specifically, the maintenance of a rigid social hierarchy which insured a monopoly over land. 
	4. This amassing of wealth meant that those newly-rich sought more political influence and the new commercial class filled political office all over Greece. 


Plato as an Aristocrat 
1. Plato was born and raised as an aristocrat. While it is unfair to dismiss Plato as an apologist for the failing aristocratic class in Athens. We cannot deny that the foundation of his political thought can be traced to common aristocratic ideas at the time. These foundations find themselves taking shape with his conception of the *kallipolis* in the Republic. 
	1. This does not equate Plato's political position with the aristocratic class. However, certain aristocratic attitudes, such as the prejudice against the growing commercial class, cannot be overlooked. 
2. A brief outline of Plato’s aristocratic lineage
	1. If we accept the authenticity of the Seventh Letter, then Plato had political ambitions in his youth which aligned him with the oligarchic revolution at the time. 
	2. After the failure of the Thirty Tyrants, he faced certain disillusionment with the re-institution of democracy. Plato claims that the Athenian "State was no longer managed according to the principles and institutions of our forefathers [...] Moreover, both the written laws and the customs were being corrupted, and that with surprising rapidity." Plato Letters, Wood1978
	3. Plato states himself that the polis is undergoing some kind of 'corruption'. This is in line with other representations of the polis written by aristocratic authors. For example:
		1. There is a quote along the lines of "The youth should not enter the polis because they come out corrupted" but I cannot find it right now. 
	4. This attitude towards the polis was one that was generally held by Aristocrats.
		1. After Pericles' death, Athenian politics was fully undergoing a transformation away from the aristocratic class. The fallout from the Peloponnesian war caused an influx of peasantry into the city (many of worked as wage-labourers, further bolstering the commercial class) and destroyed much of the aristocrats wealth. (The Greek State, Ehrenberg)
3. Aristocratic Nostalgia 
	1. Much has been written about the so-called decline of the Athenian polis. 
		1. Aristophanes characterises democracy as the leadership of "small officials and cunning beggars" (Aristophanes, Frogs)
	2. Ehrenberg characterises this change in behaviour as a shift from 'political man' to 'economic man' (The Greek State, Ehrenberg pp 21)
	3. This forms an important part of Plato's motive in writing the Republic. He views Athens as something which requires curing and he utilises this historical nostalgia of an aristocratic polis in doing so. 


To Summarise: We have developed a history of the Greek aristocracy. Tracing its beginnings and the reasons it has diminished. Two prominent reasons for this was the mass adoption of coinage and the rise of a commercial class. The latter of which displaced an aristocratic stronghold by refocusing political participation in terms of wealth instead of status.

Plato seeks to establish the aristocracy within the guardian class. The influence of money has disrupted the position of the aristocracy within Athens. Plato is vary of the power of money within politics (buying power), commodification of knowledge (sophists), hoarding, usury and predatory debt-bondage, along with its ability to force someone to develop a taste for luxury. Therefore, he seeks to contain money and its contact with the guardian class. This contextualises his ideal society in the Republic and explains his hierarchy. 

Plato's construction of the guardian class in relation to money is a historically informed decision. 

Money holds the power to disrupt politics. It enables a bunch of stuff (private property etc...) which has the power to subvert the polis. Furthermore, Plato inherits the general Athenian aristocratic attitude which associates trade, and the free flowing of money with deceit.
