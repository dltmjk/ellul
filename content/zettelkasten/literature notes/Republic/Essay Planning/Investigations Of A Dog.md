Homer didn't have money. We know this because within both the Iliad and the Odyssey there are ten distinct types of *exchange*. R. Seaford lists every one of these in his "Money and The Early Greek Mind". As we descend down the list, the examples start to resemble our current conception of trade in which two things are *equivalent in value*. Swords are exchanged for spears, goats are exchanged for cattle, and gifts are exchanged for promises of friendship. Od. 21.35, Od. 15.52–5, Il. 11.244–5. 

This exercise clarifies two things. Firstly, Greece in this period was a community of gift-exchange {1}. Money, as we know it, had not come into existence. Secondly, Metals did not yet represent an abstract, minted, measure of value that could be exchanged for commodities (Seaford, EGM). In fact, at this point in time the only real general measure of value was that of cattle which was represented by the Homeric talent {2}. 

This was a barter-economy, and therefore, pre-money. Exchange followed a pre-set social logic which was established by custom and not law. This stands in contrast to later Greece, for example in the 4th century BCE, where one is able to exchange coinage (minted representation of money), for virtually anything including "friends and honour" (Sophocles fr. 88). Since exchange was mediated through established patterns of social relations, it seems necessary to investigate the structure of society in which these social relations were forged. Which we will do through the lens of class. This decision is made in light of our goal to investigate the aristocracy. A group we argue constitutes a distinct class even in the context of pre-monetary Greek society. 

In performing this class-centred historiography, we have to defend the idea that Greece, in antiquity, contained discrete groups that were vulnerable to examination by class analysis. To justify our choice we adopt a conception of class which has been admixed with Finley's (PBUH) conception of status. We follow from G. E. M. de Ste. Croix (PBUH) in defining class as a social relationship between man and production. This materialises in the form of legal relations concerning property or labour. 

In his words "When the conditions of production, such as they are at any given time, are controlled by a particular group [...], then we have a 'class society'" (GEM pp 32). 

Therefore, the main determinant of a persons class is their relationship to the productive capabilities of a given society. Which at this point in Greece was primarily land, or the ownership of it. Ste. Croix continues "an individual’s class position in Antiquity is the extent to which he exploits the labour of others (mainly unfree) or is himself exploited” (GEM pp 270) {3}. Hence, we assert that class exists in any society in which there are material divisions between those who produce and those who control production. Greece, falling under this, is open to analysis from a class front. 

If we quickly return to pre-monetary Greece, we can easily slot certain groups into their equivalent classes. Within Homer, society was based around agriculture, controlled by a 'warrior-aristocracy' who held a monopoly on both land and war, in which group-membership was ensured by "noble birth" (Starr, _The Ancient Greeks_, p. 120). The description of this class seems to align well with our conception of of an aristocratic class. It's important to highlight that one's birth determined had an explicit relationship with one's class which is why we integrate a notion of status into our class-framework. On calculating a mans status, Finley prioritises "rights, privileges claims and duties” and if “he $[has]$ certain civil rights but no political rights" in which the combination of these attributes decided a mans place within society (AE, 61). 

This will be especially relevant in our later treatment of Plato's Athens. In which groups of rich traders (neoploutoi) are still looked upon with disdain by a separate aristocratic class. In the latter case, their status was legitimatised through "signs of distinction" (luxury goods and practices) (Kurke, Cultural Poetics in Archaic Greece). 

Those warrior-aristocrats had now fused into military aristocracy that held control over the land and the courts. Because of their birth they were the "Kaloi Kagathoi" (beautiful and good) and exemplars of *arete* (excellence) who maintained their closed circle of power by only marrying other aristocrats (Trade and Class 5th Century). It is at this time that is becomes possible to identify the rise of a distinct class culture epitomised by a "proud independence, disdain for labour and the nouveau riche, polished manner, sophisticated form and style of conduct, speech, and apparel, and devotion to sport, music and dancing." (EMW pp 2). 

Our insistence in employing the tools of class-analysis proves useful in explaining the formation of distinct class-based 'ideological attitude'. If we were deprived of this, our survey would turn a blind eye to analysing the cultural convictions held by opposing classes, limiting our later scrutiny of Plato. We have to keep in mind that one of our main goals is to establish that Plato held certain aristocratic views. In order to accomplish this we need to justify that aristocrats held cultural prejudices different from the peasants, traders, and slaves. 

We are not claiming that the entirety of Plato's political philosophy was downstream of an aristocratic impulse, or that he was a simplistic apologist for the aristocratic class. In reading the Republic, Statesman, and Laws we are presented with a layered view of politics that cannot be reduced down into a single political standpoint. However, we cannot ignore that certain characteristic aristocratic attitudes find their way into Plato's philosophy.  For example, the disdain of democracy, or the tyranny of the majority. There was a palpable aversion to both democracy and civil life within the aristocracy. According to Ellen Meiksins Wood "many of the nobles considered it to be a sign of gentlemanly virtue to remain aloof and detatched from civic life, a trend culminating in the aristocratic Epicurus withdrawal into the Garden" (EMW pp3). Likewise, Plato's treatment of democracy is at best sceptical, and at worst disgust. His portrait of democracy within the Republic (555b-569c) is of a system that will necessarily collapse into tyranny. A characterisation which seems bizarre in comparison to the existing, stable, democracy within Athens (GEM ste croix). 

Views, like the one Plato held on democracy, form an ideological-core of beliefs which Plato seeks to rationalise through his political philosophy. In reforming the polis, Plato presents a kallipolis that embodies and justifies the aristocratic 'ideological-core'. 

In the period between a pre-monetary Greece and Plato's Athens, the aristocratic class faced several defeats. Solon and Cleisthenes established new property classes which re-framed political participation around wealth, instead of birth. The Peloponnesian war had massacred the countryside which led to widespread immigration of the peasantry into the city, turning them into wage-labourers and the diversifying economy gave rise to a class of rich traders who were far more active in politics (GEM, ste, de croix). 

Given this decline, a large portion of the aristocratic class felt nostalgic for a historical Athens centred around them. We argue that Plato shared in this nostalgia and argued for it within his dialogues the *Timaeus* and the *Critias*. 






Within the *Timaeus* and the *Critias*, Plato, through the interlocutor Critias {4}, describes a conflict between two cities: A 'proto-Athens' (A fictionalisation of historical Athens) and Atlantis {5}. To recount briefly - Atlantis, a large seafaring nation, goes to war with proto-Athens, an example of Socrates's ideal city. The latter wins but both are eventually destroyed by a large natural disaster. There is an obvious ethical lesson in Plato's exposition, but the myth is far too detailed to only serve as a moral exercise. 

In our further interpretation will not treat proto-Athens as a model of what Plato *actually* thought historical Athens was like. Neither will we read the dialogue as a work of pure fiction. Instead we follow in the belief that it is a pastiche of history built out of historical elements (C Gill, The Genre of the Atlantis Story). Representing a type of myth that can be read allegorically. We argue that the dialogue serves as an argument for the superiority of historical Athens. Not because Plato draws an equivalence between proto-Athens and historical Athens but instead because he draws one between Atlantis and current Athens. 

Julia Annas gives a description of the ways in which Atlantis resembles Athens (Julia Annas Atlantis): 

"The Atlanteans have abundance of everything they need. But they are restless and seek to go beyond what they have. They build bridges and dig canals to join their city to the sea; they build docks and harbours and become traders, till the great harbour is full of the din of merchant ships coming and going. Their temples are massive and ostentatious. At first they obey their laws and hold virtue to be more important than wealth, but in time are led by greed and their power to subordinate virtue to wealth and show." Julia Annas Atlantis pp 54

Further parallel were brought out by Vidal-Naquet in noting that "Cleisthenes' reforms divided Athens into ten tribes, and it is into ten parts that Poseidon divides his own domain" (pp 274). He highlights Plato's "description of the harbors and their fortifications is greatly indebted, as has often been noted, to the complex Kantharos, Zea, Mounychia, the naval yards, and the arsenal" (Vidal Naquet, pp 275). From this, he asserts that proto-Athens "was the antithesis of the real city of the fifth and fourth centuries-in a word, an anti-Athens. (Black Hunter pp 270). 

Ancient readers of Critias would've spotted the parallels between between Atlantis and Athens. The most obvious of which was through reference to the Peloponnesian War, in which the Athenian conquest of Sicily ended in defeat, resulting in a loss of her former subjects (Cite Julia). The resemblance is made explicit by the presence of Hermocrates, the general considered responsible for gathering an anti-Athens coalition. Given these repeated instances, we can say that there is a deliberate intention by Plato to relate Atlantis and modern Athens.

Pitting Athens against its much younger counterpart serves an explicit ideological purpose. Especially when we consider that this younger nation is an example of Socrates's *kallipolis*, an idea which was formulated for the first time only a day before this dialogue takes place. It doesn't seem to be a coincidence that Plato puts forward his conception of the ideal state and immediately uses a primeval Athens as its concrete implementation. The comparison serves to critique modern Athens and bolster the idea of historical Athens. 

Given this reading of the dialogues, it seems like Plato was tacitly endorsing a view that we should return to the "good old days". Plato's account of the Persian attack on the Greeks within his *Laws 698b-700a* could also be read as a please to return to Solon's constitution, by associating the constitution with Lady Modesty and their victory of Marathon (cite Julia). Isocrates, in his Panegyricus, provides the most clear example of this aristocratic nostalgia for an 'ancestral constitution' (Panegyricus, 39–40, 66–68, 78–79). A sentimentality, we argue, Plato approves of. In our later treatment of the Republic we argue that Plato constructs his *kallipolis* in a manner which resembles this ancestral Athens. 

After its defeat, Atlantis is slowly corrupted - not least by the influence of wealth and money - and eventually destroyed by Zeus's wrath. The scale of which also devastates Athens, splitting it into islands which, according to Vidal-Nasquet, "condemn[s them] to seafaring and all that involves - political change, commerce, imperialism" (PVN, Black Hunter 272)". The transition of proto-Athens from a large landed acropolis to a seafaring state seems to be Plato drawing a historical link towards modern Athens. 

If we follow Vidal-Nasquet's analysis, the denigration of Athens begins with a state in flux, increasingly concerned with trade and imperialism. In our next section we trace a brief history of the former, focusing on how the class generated by this activity - a commercial class - displaces the aristocracy.



Coins and Trade

"So when currency had been now invented as an outcome of the necessary interchange of goods, there came into existence the other form of wealth-getting, trade, which at first no doubt went on in a simple form, but later became more highly organized as experience discovered the sources and methods of exchange that would cause most profit" Aristot. Pol. 1.1257b

Aegina was the first city to issue its own coinage. Quick to follow were Athens and Corinth. Eventually it was massively adopted, which spurred trade and a newly minted class of merchants. These merchants traders were given specific names relating to their trade, there were ship-owners (naukleros), rope-merchants (lysicles), cattle-merchants (cleon), and grain dealers (eucrates). We will refer to this group as the commercial class. These were not members of the aristocracy, some weren't even citizens {6} . However, the rise of coinage, along with demand overseas and a decline in piracy allowed for a set of non-aristocrats to engage in trade and become wealthy \footcite{glassman2017trade}. 

Their wealth allowed them entry into politics. Leading to a generation of Greek political figures who trace their lineage back to merchants and craftsmen. The members of this class held antagonistic interests towards the previously dominant aristocrats. The main arenas of this conflict was the recognition of alternate civic authorities, between the symposium and the polis or agora. von Reden argues that the "Recognition of coinage as a recompense meant the acknowledgement of the polis as an institution that controlled justice and prosperity." (Exchange in Ancient Greece by Sitta Von Reden, p.175). 

She contends that coinage "does not by nature signify anything particular - economic relationships, egalitarianism, the market, etc. - but is symbolised by its repeated usage in particular institutions," (von Reden, p.154). The rise of coinage giving way to a politically active commercial class created strong associations between the two and their place of business. Coinage represents a threat to the aristocratic class. It's form as a precious metal can be traced back to the context of aristocratic-gift exchange. In appropriating this, the city now is the dominant form in the circulation of these metals which displaces the aristocracy. 

Furthermore, coinage, in acting as a universal abstract indicator of value, breaks down previous hierarchies of exchange. It makes labour a measurable activity and comparable, therefore challenging the class interests of the aristocrats (Brohannan 1959).

This shifting influence was met with pullback by the aristocratic class. According to Leslie Kurke, this materialised in maintaining a "parapolitical system based on the interlocking institutions of *xenia* and *hetaireia*" in which this "system of xenia could often be in tension, if not in open conflict, with the demands of the local authority of the polis" (Kurke, CBG, p. 17). Their bitterness towards coinage is best displayed in their treatment of the *kapeleia*. Extending from von Reden's point about coinage being intertwined with its usage in certain contexts. The meaning of *kapeleia* is closely associated with the concept of coinage. Leslie Kurke traces a history of the term as arriving from the east to signify a "petty retailer". She gives the following examples of this association in play: 

Within Herodotus Kyros says the following: "I have never yet feared such men, for whom there is a place appointed in the middle of the city for them to come together and deceive each other on oath" Hdt.1.153.1

τοῖσί ἐστι χώρος ἐν μέσῃ τῇ πόλι ἀποδεδειγμένος ἐς τὸν συλλεγόμενοι ἀλλήλους ὀμνύντες ἐξαπατῶσι

Herodotus continues “all the Greeks, because, having set up agoras, they practice buying and selling” (ὅτι ἀγορὰς στησάμενοι ὠνῇ τε καὶ πώλη χρῶνται, Hdt. 1.153.2). 

According to Diogenes Laertius, Anacharsis “defined the agora as a place for deceiving each other and claiming more than one’s share” (τὴν ἀγορὰν ὁρισαμένου ἔφη τόπον εἶς τὸ ἀλλήλους ἀπατᾶν καὶ πλεονεκτεῖν, D.L. 1.105).

Plato gives his description of the *kapeleia* within the Republic: 

Adeimantus: [...] They are generally those whose bodies are weakest and who are not fit to do any other sort of work. Their job is to wait there in the marketplace and exchange money for the goods of those who have something sell [...]

Socrates: [...] Those who wait in the marketplace, and provide this service of buying and selling, are called retailers (kapeloi), aren't they

Plato Rep. 371c

The characterisation by Adeimantus seems to align Plato with an aristocratic disdain for the kapeloi. An attitude which frames the agora, and those who partake in its business, as deceitful and weak. We can read the treatment of the agora and the kapeloi as reflecting the aristocratic attitude towards coinage. Returning to von Reden, coinage is symbolised by its usage by certain places and people. In their contempt of the kapeloi and the agora, the aristocratic class approaches coinage with the same attitude.
