## Introduction 
1. How did Mao-era China responded to major epizootic and zoonotic diseases 
	1. For example, 
		1. Anthrax, swine fever, foot-and-mouth disease (FMD), brucellosis, and so on
	2. The general pattern in fighting them was called the "Comprehensive Prevention and Treatment" (CPT)
		1. This included lockdowns, quarantines, disinfection, mass animal vaccination, mass education, and prioritizing the treatment of infected animals over mass culling.
2. Historians of Modern China emphasize that self reliance is one of the central features of Maoism
	1. For example, autonomous from imperial powers
	> Chinese Communist Party’s (CCP) style of governance or domestic policy that foregrounded “the proletarian revolutionary spirit of using local methods, starting from scratch, . . . not fearing failure, and overcoming hardships.”
3. China hoped to extract surplus resources from rural China and channel it back into state industrialization. 
	> What we come to see at least in the realm of animal health is the figure of the state, which was ideologically interventionist yet materially inactive, and that of grassroots communes, which became capable of fighting animal infectious diseases on their own—represented by the institutionalization of the CPT—despite their ideological subordination to the state.

## Collectives, Animal Diseases, and Veterinary Workers
1. Maoist agrarian collectivization started in 1953, four years after coming to power. 
	> The goal was to maximize the state’s capacity to control and extract an agrarian surplus for the sake of primitive accumulation to support rapid industrialization
2. Villages were transformed into basic collective units called "mutual aid teams"
	1. The collective became a unit of production and distribution. 
		> The scale rose throughout the decade. On average, seven villages (production teams) formed an “advanced cooperative” or a “production brigade” that consisted of about 220 households and 980 people. After 1958, the largest cooperatives, “people’s communes,” appeared and boasted an average population of 15,000
3. There wasn't much top-down state pressure. There were veterinary assistants who weren't vets but organized lockdown/quarantine measures along with diagnosing animals. 
	1. These bottom-up actions were called CPT
