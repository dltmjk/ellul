Notes
1. Necessary Trust View 
	1. Scientific knowledge production is characterised by epistemic dependence 
	2. Sometimes epistemic checks aren't possible, which is why trust is necessary. 
		1. Scientists must also trust that the decisions their colleagues make in scientific research are dependable and acceptable. 
	3. Trust is unavoidable. 
		1. "Many practices in science are designed so as to minimise the need for trust; scientific communities control the quality of the produced knowledge through critical discussions, peer review and training."
		2. However, a degree of trust is still required. 
		3. A wide network of people police these epistemic responsibilities 
2. What is trust?
	1. Trust vs Reliance [[Trust Vs Reliance]]
		1. Trust can be betrayed, reliance can only be disappointed. 
	2. Thick Trust
		1.  I will call "thick" the accounts of trust that emphasise the moral and/or affective elements of trust, and therefore necessarily take trust to be an appropriate attitude only towards agents.
		2. E.g. trust towards friends 
	3. Thin Trust
		1. "Thin accounts that do not emphasise the moral and/or affective dimensions of trust. They often do not make such a sharp distinction between trust and reliance, and/or they do not require the trustee to be an agent"
3. Epistemic dependence on instruments 
	1. Only agents can be recipients of trust 
	2. Instruments as a level of "quasi-trust"
		1. Based on Bruno Latour's idea of nonhuman actants 
			1. There are normative expectations of an instruments functioning
				1. But where do these normative expectations arise from?
			2. Quasi-trust cannot be moral 
					1. Is this different from reliance?
					2. How is reliance in an instrument formed?
						1. Trust in the manufacturer and standards that an instrument was made according to
	3. Epistemically justified reliance
