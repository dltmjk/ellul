===Training DNN Thing===
Due to how different the training and deployment stage are, Boge (2022), along with others (Sullivan 2022a, Skogaard 2020), introduced two ways in which a DNN can gain χ. He makes a distinction between h(ow)-opacity, which concerns the way in which a DNN automatically alters the instantiated function in response to data” (p. 59), and w(hat)-opacity which concerns what function was learned in the course of training. 

Sogaard (2023) makes a similar division between inference and training opacity. The former relies on a human agent being unable to relate input and output. Whereas the latter is caused when a human agent "cannot, upon inspection, say how, in general, the parameters of the DNN were induced as a result of its training data" (Søgaard, 2023, p. 225). According to Sogaard, ppacity presents problems to a human agent who is unable to explain how the model ended up with a certain configuration. However, if we accept the T-DNN as a causal instrument, there are other ways we can explain the output without having to appeal to any higher-level logical rules. 

To defend this, we must ask what the epistemically relevant parts of a causal instrument are? If we return to the microscope its epistemic justification does not rest upon our knowledge of the precise causal laws that govern every optical interaction within its lenses. The microscope is justified as a scientific instrument because we can reliably trace the function of its working parts and observe that it consistently produces representations of microscopic phenomena under specified conditions. The reliability of the microscope’s causal chain, for example the alignment of its lenses, to the refraction of light, to the stability of its mechanical structure, grounds our trust in the images it delivers. Therefore, epistemic relevancy concerns the reliability of each working part doing what is was constructed to do, along with the contexts of use (what the microscope is identifying). They are justified based on their ability to perform to pick up on a causal relationship repeatedly - in the case of a T-DNN, this is finding a certain function from a function space. 

Similarly, it is proven that DNNs are universal function approximators (source), therefore they will always pick up on a certain function. This is known just from the architecture of the network: the network’s layers, activation functions, and optimisation process collectively constitute its “working parts.” When properly constructed and trained, these components interact to produce stable mappings from input to output across a range of data. Therefore, for an agent with this knowledge, the DNN is perfectly transparent. As a causal instrument, what needs to be maintained is the relationship between parts and the reliability of the parts themselves (which are doing what they are specified to). 

In other words, while constructing the model, the T-DNN finds features within the input space and fits onto certain patterns inherent within the data. Some argue (cite) that we require access to the internal rules that a T-DNN utilises when constructing a function in order to for the process to be truly reliable. This is so we have an algorithmically transparent training stage. However, this is only a reasonable demand if algorithmic transparency is the only way to increase the reliability of a T-DNN. We do not need to appeal to algorithmic transparency, because we are fully capable of conferring reliability in order ways. Similar to other causal instruments that were built without a full understanding of what function they were picking up on, we can justify their usage by giving reasons for why every subpart is reliable. 

Any appeal towards higher-level explanations are not required. We can show this if we return to our formulation of the construction of causal instruments, which shows that they do not require a theoretical backbone. The construction of the microscope did not require a fully formed theory of heat in order to justify its results. An argument along the form of "Galileo did not have access to Black's theory of latent heat, which is what his thermoscope followed, therefore he could not justify the results". Access to the underlying law an instrument may be following in producing a result is irrelevant for discussion of reliability - what is epistemically relevant is that the instrument performs the same function consistently. 

There is one way in which the relevant theory of a DNN is the actual math behind the updating parameters. However, the type of theory we are interested in is the one the T-DNN fits onto using these parameters. For example, in constructing a DNN which mimics Newton's law of gravitation, we are interested in Newton's law - not the theory of DNN architecture. We know that the DNN picks up on this relationship, but we don't require knowledge of it to create T-DNNs which pick up on it better than another, more generic, T-DNN. 

Some will argue that because a T-DNN can occasionally pick up on the "incorrect" function and this presents problems with reliability. For example, adversarial attacks and overfitting. The former describes a phenomenon in which DNNs are vulnerable to "deceptions" (Papernot et al. 2016a; Goodfellow et al. 2015; Szegedy et al. 2014). For example, certain DNNs "cheat" their way through a classification. Consider a neural network trained to classify MRI images as containing a tumour or not. Suppose that in the training data, scans showing tumours were disproportionately captured under certain lighting conditions or scanner settings (e.g. brighter room lighting, particular scanner gain). The network might learn to associate brightness or shading artefacts with the tumour label. Meaning that it “recognising lighting conditions” rather than actual tumour morphology (Athalye 2017). Similarly "overfitting" is a model's capacity to fit strongly on the training data, which prevents generalisation. Would being privy to the higher-level rules of a DNNs training fix this? 

For example, if we consider a compass produced and utilised within a scientific experiment. We are hoping that it picks up on the earths magnetic fields, however other strong magnetic forces may interfere. The compass is doing what is was produced for - picking up on a magnetic field. Similar to a DNN which picks up on a certain function based on patterned features within the input data. It is impossible to control that it will pick up on the correct function every-time because the context of what the "correct" function is will change. Similar to if a researcher wants to use a compass to measure the magnetic field of an object in another room, instead of the earth's. Similarly, the microscope is a magnifier, what it actually magnifies is variable. Adversarial attacks and over-fitting would cause problems with reliability if there was no way to shift these contexts of us. Fortunately, causal instruments and DNNs, both have other methods which confer reliability based on enforcing or regulating certain epistemically relevant parts. 

In our previous case of tumour classification, a greater variety of training data would be able to rectify the error. The medical‐imaging literature is alert to this: artefacts (scanner noise, motion blur, background gradients) are known to be confounders and are often controlled or corrected in preprocessing or artifact‐removal pipelines (see Wide Range MRI Artifact Removal with Transformers). For the reliability of the training stage of the DNN we only need to defer to the contexts of its experimental use. What we are interested in is picking up on the *correct* pattern. In attempting to increase the reliability of this occurring, researchers can utilise pragmatic statistical and scientific methods. 

Kieval and Westerblad (CITE) advance a type of method-learning, inspired by Dewey’s view that methods emerge “organically” from successful inquiry and then serve as standards for future work. This sort of scientific-know how means that the T-DNN stage reliability depends on epistemically relevant elements apart from the actual model that grow out of scientific practice. Method-learning, and its constituents, is how agents can justify the reliability of an instrument. Instead of appealing to a wholly transparent argument, they appeal to methods such as design-rules, and other epistemic strategies for justification of reliability. 

"We know that some methods of inquiry are better than others in just the same way in which we know that some methods of surgery, farming, road-making, navigating or what-not are better than others" Dewey, 1938: 108)

This type of treatment is common for computer simulations. Lenhard (2006, 2009, method) argues that, in the case of an opaque computer simulation, agents can grasp the mechanics of a system through its action instead of its theoretical backbone. 

If we return to our AlphaFold example. the training process was guided by expert knowledge of biological constraints (CITE alphafold). Which were encoded as inductive biases and geometric constraints, rather than by theoretical principles of protein folding. This sort of steering of a DNNs architecture allows an agent to argue, with evidence, good reasons for the reliability of its prediction. Predictions which do not depend on transparent reasoning but on the successful articulation of methods that mediate learning.
