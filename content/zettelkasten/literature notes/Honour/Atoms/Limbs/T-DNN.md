This section argues that we can justify the T-DNN’s reliability without appealing to its algorithmic transparency, thereby avoiding problems associated with opacity. We begin by outlining how reliability is conferred upon a standard causal instrument. Next, we examine whether applying these epistemic criteria poses any problems for a T-DNN. We conclude by proposing a form of “method-learning” in the use of T-DNNs.

===Normal Causal Instruments===
To start, how do we justify the results of a normal causal instrument? If we return to the microscope its epistemic justification does not rest upon our knowledge of the precise causal laws that govern every optical interaction within its lenses. The microscope is justified as a scientific instrument because we can reliably trace the function of its working parts and observe that it consistently produces representations of microscopic phenomena under specified conditions. The reliability of the microscope’s causal chain, for example the alignment of its lenses, to the refraction of light, to the stability of its mechanical structure, grounds our trust in the images it delivers. Therefore, epistemic relevancy concerns the reliability of each working part doing what it was constructed to do, along with the contexts of use (i.e, what the microscope is being used to identify) \cite{baird}. 

One might argue that the reliability of the constituent parts does not confer reliability on the whole. This is a genuine concern because we still have to give reasoning for why the instrument works as intended. However, explanations of this type do not need to appeal to anything more than certain mechanical behaviours within the instrument. That is, we do not need to defer to any higher-level rules which the microscope follows (such as optical laws). Within a microscope, for example, we can provide an explanation of the sort: “the condenser lens focuses the light into a narrow beam, increasing the illumination of the target region.” This explanation does not appeal to the theory underlying the microscope but rather to its material functionality, and, despite this, it still counts as a genuine reason for justifying reliability. Therefore, epistemically justifying the results of a causal instrument can be done if it is structurally transparent.

===Previous Literature===
The type of opacity relevant to DNNs is algorithmic opacity. Authors who have previously distinguished between the training and deployment stages focus on how the conditions of opacity differ between them. Boge (2022), along with others (Søgaard, 2020), introduced two ways in which a DNN can gain χ. He makes a distinction between h(ow)-opacity, which concerns the way in which a DNN automatically alters the instantiated function in response to data (p. 59), and w(hat)-opacity, which concerns what function was learned in the course of training.

Søgaard (2023) makes a similar distinction between inference and training opacity. The former relies on a human agent being unable to relate input and output, whereas the latter arises when a human agent "cannot, upon inspection, say how, in general, the parameters of the DNN were induced as a result of its training data" (Søgaard, 2023, p. 225). Both Boge and Søgaard argue that the opacity of the training process presents problems for reliability. However, we contend that this is a case of misattributing epistemic responsibility onto the T-DNN.

If we can confer reliability based on purely structural transparency, then can we not do the same for DNNs? The immediate problem is that we cannot reduce the theory-dependence of a T-DNN in the same way we did with a microscope. A microscope relies on the laws of optics, whereas a DNN relies on the results of SLT. The more important theory, as Boge asserts, is the one that the T-DNN “realises” in constructing its function. Our lack of epistemic access to this theory is an important caveat that limits our reliance on these systems. If reliability can only be conferred when an agent has direct knowledge of this theory, then opacity presents serious problems. We argue that T-DNNs, as a type of causal instrument, can be epistemically justified for reasons that are not obscured by algorithmic opacity. This seems intuitively appealing: if a T-DNN is the same type of instrument as a microscope, then the epistemic demands of both should be equal. To demonstrate that this holds, we first need to delineate the actual function of the T-DNN.

The architecture of the T-DNN has already been covered in Section 1. The network’s layers, activation functions, and optimisation process collectively constitute its “working parts.” When properly constructed and trained, these components interact to produce stable mappings from input to output across a range of data. The code implements these features on a computer, and because programming languages are structurally transparent, so is the T-DNN. While constructing the model, the T-DNN identifies features within the input space and fits certain patterns inherent in the data.

When justifying the reliability of a normal causal instrument, we provide explanations that refer to how its subparts interact. It is important to note that these explanations do not rely on any higher-level rules but are grounded solely in the architecture of the system. Why, then, would an explanation that invokes only the structurally transparent features of the T-DNN fail?

The argument is that because a T-DNN can occasionally pick up on the “incorrect” function, which presents problems for reliability. Examples include adversarial attacks and overfitting. Adversarial attacks describe a phenomenon in which DNNs are vulnerable to “deceptions” (Papernot et al., 2016a; Goodfellow et al., 2015; Szegedy et al., 2014). For instance, certain DNNs can “cheat” their way through classification. Consider a neural network trained to classify MRI images as containing a tumour or not. Suppose that in the training data, scans showing tumours were disproportionately captured under certain lighting conditions or scanner settings (e.g., brighter room lighting, particular scanner gain). The network might then learn to associate brightness or shading artefacts with the tumour label. In other words, it is “recognizing lighting conditions” rather than actual tumour morphology (Athalye, 2017). Similarly, overfitting occurs when a model fits too closely to the training data, which prevents generalisation. Would access to the higher-level rules of a DNN’s training resolve this issue?

The answer is broadly yes. However, it seems an appeal to higher-level rules is contentious because causal instruments are not capable of providing explanations of this type. These tools are employed within specific experimental contexts to produce results rather than to make claims. For example, a microscope measures a causal relationship within a phenomenon but does not make claims about the phenomenon itself. Should the same demand be placed on a T-DNN?

What Boge and Søgaard require is access to the function that the T-DNN implements. 

Furthermore, reasons given by an agent to explain why a certain output was produced often refer to circumstances outside the functioning of the system. For example, if you want to explain why a spectrometer produced a certain output, you would appeal to the input data. A T-DNN is, after all, a tool that minimises loss in relation to input data. If we can give reasons that support the consistency of this function, we can sustain accounts of reliability. We acknowledge that access to its “decision-making” process would enhance explanatory potential, but reliability can still be conferred with or without this feature. 

Returning to our problem of adversarial attacks. Let's consider a compass produced and utilised within a scientific experiment, we hope that it picks up on the Earth’s magnetic field. However, other strong magnetic forces may interfere. The compass is doing what it was produced for - picking up on a magnetic field. This is similar to a DNN, which picks up on a certain function based on patterned features within the input data. It is impossible to control that it will pick up on the correct function every time because the context of what the "correct" function is will change. Similar to if a researcher wants to use a compass to measure the magnetic field of an object in another room, instead of the Earth's. Similarly, the microscope is a magnifier; what it actually magnifies is variable. Adversarial attacks and overfitting would cause problems with reliability if there was no way to shift these contexts of use. Fortunately, causal instruments and DNNs, both have other methods which confer reliability. 

In our previous case of tumour classification, a greater variety of training data would be able to rectify the error. The medical‐imaging literature is alert to this: artefacts (scanner noise, motion blur, background gradients) are known to be confounders and are often controlled or corrected in preprocessing or artifact‐removal pipelines (see Wide Range MRI Artifact Removal with Transformers). For the reliability of the training stage of the DNN we only need to defer to the contexts of its experimental use. What we are interested in is picking up on the *correct* pattern. In attempting to increase the likelihood of this occurring, researchers can utilise pragmatic statistical and scientific methods. It's important to note that these methods do not require any appeal to algorithmic transparency in the T-DNN. They function, and can be justified, on the basis of structural transparency (i.e., the parameters of the DNN)

Kieval and Westerblad (CITE) advance a type of method-learning, inspired by Dewey’s view that methods emerge “organically” from successful inquiry and then serve as standards for future work. This sort of scientific know-how means that the T-DNN stage reliability depends on epistemically relevant elements apart from the actual model that grow out of scientific practice. Method-learning, and its constituents, are how agents can justify the reliability of an instrument. Instead of appealing to a wholly transparent argument, they appeal to methods such as design-rules and other epistemic strategies for justification of reliability. 

> "We know that some methods of inquiry are better than others in just the same way in which we know that some methods of surgery, farming, road-making, navigating or what-not are better than others" Dewey, 1938: 108)

Lenhard (2006, 2009, method-learning) argues that, in the case of an opaque computer simulation, agents can grasp the mechanics of a system through its action instead of its theoretical backbone. If we return to our AlphaFold example, the training process was guided by expert knowledge of biological constraints (CITE AlphaFold), which were encoded as inductive biases and geometric constraints rather than by theoretical principles of protein folding. This sort of steering of a DNN’s architecture allows an agent to argue, with evidence, for the reliability of its predictions. These predictions do not depend on transparent reasoning but on the successful articulation of methods that mediate learning. The model’s epistemic legitimacy, then, parallels that of a scientific instrument whose calibration is maintained through expert practice. 

To sum: while access to a T-DNN’s higher-level ‘decision-making’ process would be useful, we can give good reasons for the reliability of a T-DNN without appealing to it. Some may prefer explanations referring to the former, taking the form “the DNN used this feature because it has x relevancy.” Other explanations, for example those pertaining to the input data, such as “our model shows robust performance in an i.i.d. setting” \cite{Grote}, work equally well.










Similarly, it is proven that DNNs are universal function approximators (source), therefore they will always pick up on a certain function. This is known just from the architecture of the network: the network’s layers, activation functions, and optimisation process collectively constitute its “working parts.” When properly constructed and trained, these components interact to produce stable mappings from input to output across a range of data. Therefore, for an agent with this knowledge, the DNN is perfectly transparent. As a causal instrument, what needs to be maintained is the relationship between parts and the reliability of the parts themselves (which are doing what they are specified to). 

In other words, while constructing the model, the T-DNN finds features within the input space and fits onto certain patterns inherent within the data. Some argue (cite) that we require access to the internal rules that a T-DNN utilises when constructing a function in order to for the process to be truly reliable. This is so we have an algorithmically transparent training stage. However, this is only a reasonable demand if algorithmic transparency is the only way to increase the reliability of a T-DNN. We do not need to appeal to algorithmic transparency, because we are fully capable of conferring reliability in order ways. Similar to other causal instruments that were built without a full understanding of what function they were picking up on, we can justify their usage by giving reasons for why every subpart is reliable. 

Any appeal towards higher-level explanations are not required. We can show this if we return to our formulation of the construction of causal instruments, which shows that they do not require a theoretical backbone. The construction of the microscope did not require a fully formed theory of heat in order to justify its results. An argument along the form of "Galileo did not have access to Black's theory of latent heat, which is what his thermoscope followed, therefore he could not justify the results". Access to the underlying law an instrument may be following in producing a result is irrelevant for discussion of reliability - what is epistemically relevant is that the instrument performs the same function consistently. 

There is one way in which the relevant theory of a DNN is the actual math behind the updating parameters. However, the type of theory we are interested in is the one the T-DNN fits onto using these parameters. For example, in constructing a DNN which mimics Newton's law of gravitation, we are interested in Newton's law - not the theory of DNN architecture.
