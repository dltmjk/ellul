To begin, we need to try and arrive at a definition of a scientific instrument. Kletzl (CITE) proposes that the category, as a whole, is an intuitive one. Even current classifications of instruments do not ground themselves on a credible epistemic principle that justifies the collection of such different tools under the singular header of a ‘scientific instrument’. For example, there is still no widely accepted rationale for placing both the microscope and the large hadron collider in the same category. Attempts to remedy this have split the concept into multiple subcategories. 

The first of these, in the 17th century, divided the label into “philosophical” and “mathematical” instruments (Taub, 2011, SS pg.73). The former concerned itself with “pure truths” about nature’s inner working, employed mainly by “natural philosophers” and ranged from including tools such as telescopes and air pumps (Cite Kletzl) (Warner 1990). In essence, these philosophical instruments were concerned with the demonstration of a hypothesis through the usage of a particular object. In comparison, “mathematical instruments” were tools associated with measurement and manipulation of constructs, such as the quadrant. Historically, the division between both was not founded on any foundational epistemic rule, instead the split grew out of certain social, such as commercial, interests (Taub, 2009).

The lack of a guiding epistemic principle for what makes an object into an instrument means that any further sub-classification was discretionary. The consequence being that that some instruments can be argued to occupy both categories of mathematical and philosophical (van Helden 1983, 65). Despite this arbitrary labelling, other, more contemporary, systems of instrument taxonomy continue to draw divisions around similar lines.

Harre (2010, Equipment for an experiment) distinguishes between instruments and apparatus. The former is causally related to the world as tools which reveal natural phenomena. For example, a litmus solution is able to reveal whether a particular mixture is acidic or base. The instrument works through _exploiting_ a causal connection with the world. Within this category are most measuring devices – e.g., rulers, thermometers, and clocks. The thermometer is widely considered the characteristic epistemological example of a scientific instrument (Citation within Kletzl “Russel”) and, like all instruments of this class, its usefulness is dependent on its ability to respond to certain changes within the world to produce a resulting phenomenon. In this case of a mercury thermometer, the mercury will expand as the temperature of its surroundings increase.

In comparison, an apparatus is a tool which stands as an abstract model of some part of the world. In attempting to study the behaviour of ocean waves under controlled circumstances, we employ a specialised water tank constructed to model the behaviour of the ocean in which the conditions within the water tank are analogous to the conditions of the ocean. While there is no causal connection between the two, knowledge is produced, and is dependent upon, our model acting as a stand-in for the relevant phenomena (Harre 2010).

Baird (2004, Thing Knowledge) draws a similar distinction between three types of scientific instruments: models; devices which create phenomenon; and measuring instruments. Models are similar to Harre’s apparatus but contains both mechanical and mathematical models and structures both objects and their relations (pg. 23). Therefore, for Baird, most models are conceptual instead of material. For example, Mathematics models specify relations between objects through equations, such as the law of gravitation F=Gr2m1​m2​​, which relates mass and distance to the force of attraction. This broader conception of a scientific instrument will be useful in our later treatment of DNNs, as most statistical methods would be considered instruments.

Other systems of classification, such as Heidelbergers (CITE) match Baird in allowing mathematical models the status of instruments. He delineates four different roles a scientific instrument can play in the context of an experiment: Instruments that produce or split phenomena; modulating the behaviour of phenomena; imitating phenomena; and representing phenomena.

What is the purpose of this exercise? Within the differing taxonomies, we can identify certain static features of scientific instruments, along with two broad categories. Shared between every classification is a distinction between instruments that constructed in order to pick up causal relationships within the world (_causal instruments)_, and instruments that are constructed in order to model the world (_model instruments)_. Harré’s apparatuses, Baird’s measuring instruments and phenomenon-producing devices, along with Heidelberger’s instruments that generate, split, or modulate phenomena, all broadly invoke the idea that these instruments causally detect specific relationships within the world (Hacking 1983, Representing and intervening). 

In the case of _causal instruments_, the instrument takes advantage of an established causal pathway. Modern electromagnetic motors, for example, operate on a theoretical principle that a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force, as expressed by the Lorentz force law. When an electric current passes through the motor’s coil, the interaction between the generated magnetic field of the coil and the permanent (or electromagnetically induced) magnetic field of the stator produces torque, causing rotational motion (CITE).

While these principles are well understood and established now, Michael Faraday, at the time of his original experiments on electromagnetism in 1821, was unaware of these guiding laws. His construction of the electric motor was based on his theorised, and underdeveloped understanding, of electromagnetic laws. The final goal, of showing how a certain arrangement of electric and magnetic elements could produce motion, supported his theory by matching his hypothesized results (Faraday 1844, Thing Knowledge). Compared to contemporary motors, his device worked in a simpler fashion, in which a wire is suspended around a magnet allowing for the intersecting magnetic and electric fields to produce motion. The specific construction of the instrument depended on his working knowledge and the specific dynamics of the experiment. Therefore, the form an instrument takes arises out of the backdrop of theory and experiment.

Despite the lack of theoretical knowledge surrounding the behaviour of Faraday’s electromagnetic motor, the device was able to produce motion. Faraday did not have epistemic access to the underlying laws mediating the behaviour of his motor. He had a primitive theory of electromagnetism and constructed his motor around some posited causal connections. Any explanatory power came out of the instruments position within his greater theory. Strictly speaking, all the instrument did was produce motion. Explaining exactly why requires grounding in a certain theory – not necessarily a correct theory – but some sort of theory regardless.

_Model instruments_ (or just models) arise out of the same logic. However, their utility is qualitatively different as it depends on a representative function. As stated before, our treatment of these instruments follows from Baird (2004) in including mathematical formulas, such as statistical techniques or experimental methods (e.g. Randomised Control Trials). While there are both material models (Watson and Cricks “ball and stick” DNA is an example) and conceptual models. Our discussion will mainly focus on the latter. By and large, ‘a model is an interpretative description of a phenomenon that facilitates access to that phenomenon (Bailer-Jones, 2009, p.1). Therefore, models are instruments which represent their target-phenomenon and facilitate the creation of explanatory theories regarding said phenomenon (Frigg & Hartmann 2020, Weisberg 2013 – analogue model citations).

For example, if we return to our example of Faraday, the mechanics of his electromagnetic motor were empirically recorded and produced a set of mathematical models. Maxwell reformulated Faraday’s observations into a formula expressing the law of induction, expressed as such:

\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}

(Faraday, Experimental Researches in Electricity, 1839)

The model was not constructed in isolation, but in response to a set of experimental outputs. These models, now known as Maxwell’s equations, are a type of _model instrument_ that both explains and predicts phenomena (Maxwell, A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism, 1873).

These historical examples allow us to make the following claims about the usage and construction of scientific instruments. Firstly, they are produced in view of a (potentially incorrect) theory. Secondly, their explanatory quality comes out of this theory-dependence. Returning to DNNs, what type of instrument are they? To answer this, we have to look at the contexts in which they are produced and utilised.

If we return to our explanation of SLT, DNNs are constructed only in view of a supposed theoretical relationship $f$ which can be predicted through a function $h$. A dataset $D$ is generated, with certain assumptions, in order to allow the DNN to pick up on this relationship. This is the training stage of the DNN and allows for the the instruments later capability to predict on these causal relationships. If _causal instruments_ pick up latent patterns within the world, then doesn’t a DNN – by picking up on a certain function – do exactly this? For example, a DNN being trained on radiology images directly “responds” to the presence of a tumour by activating specific feature detectors and updating its internal architecture. 

Does this mean that DNNs are *causal instruments*? this type of labelling seems incorrect - after all, a DNN is mainly used to predict in a way similar to other statistical models and those are certainly not *causal instruments*. If we look at a common usage of DNN technology - weather prediction - we can see that these models (e.g. graph neural nets for atmospheric flows) learn to approximate the underlying physics of weather and can generate forecasts without directly sensing temperature, pressure, or wind. This is a standard *model instrument*. 

This tension can be resolved by acknowledging the two "parts" of a DNN: the training phase and the deployment phase. Both of which are vastly different in function. In the former example of tumour detection, we were describing the training stage of a DNN (which we will call the T-DNN). Whereas the latter example was the DNN in deployment. The T-DNN has its internal parameters modified in response to the training data, which is why we are able to claim it has a causal relationship with the world. Parameter changes depend on the specific causal structure of that data. However, in deployment the DNN no longer changes its parameters, it performs predictions based on an already constructed function and represents this causal structure in through its architecture. 

Both are performing different functions, the T-DNN needs to successfully align h with f and must be justified for reasons that contribute to its success in this domain. Whereas the deployment DNN needs to be an accurate model of the world. This relationship, and its contradictory nature, has only been described in respects to opacity (Boge Two Dimensions,Sullivan 2022a, Skogaard 2020). However, it is very relevant in evaluating how we can trust a DNN. 

We are not suggesting that DNNs are actually two scientific instruments but we must acknowledge that they contain parts that perform different functions and therefore must be justified by different criteria. When a scientific instrument encompass multiple categories, its nature of justification need to include the way that this relationship with both categories is mediated (Baird). Therefore, DNNs require different justifications for their training and deployment phase. 

We first need to answer how we can justify the usage of a normal (e.g. microscope) *scientific instrument*. A distinction needs to be drawn immediately between trust and reliance. Trust, in relation to objects, has been explored within both social epistemology and moral philosophy literature and is generally regarded as reliance along with something else (Baier, 1986; Hardin, 1996; Gerkin 2015). Reliance is a necessary but not a sufficient condition for trust. The stance we take is that trust is a type of relationship that can only be directed towards other agents who possess certain dispositions, such as acting honestly or taking responsibility for their actions (Hatherley 2020,  (Ryan, 2020 (deude instrument)).

 When we trust someone not to rob us, we are trusting in their moral conscience. Scientific instruments, which do not have emotive states or a moral conscience, cannot be the recipients of trust. They do not act meaningfully, but instead only function correctly and produce outputs. For example, when we use pencil to write, we are not trusting it to perform its function - instead we are simply relying on it. Our focus will be on how to justify reliance on an object, especially when we are epistemically dependant on its results. 

It’s generally agreed that certain core reasons are required in the justification of results from a device (Lehrer 1995; Sosa 2011, Dahl pg. 572). It should follow those different categories of instruments should demand varying types of justification. The types of reasoning we give in explaining our justification of the thermometer (an instrument under Harre, a measuring instrument according to Baird, an instrument that splits phenomena for Heidelberger, but generally a _causal instrument_) will differ from justifying our results from the usage of a wind tunnel (a _model instrument_) (Cite Duede and Baird). Both the thermometer and the wind tunnel have to be fulfilled certain criteria in order to be justified. However, the conditions that each has to meet will differ.

Sosa’s (2006, Freiman Testimony) explanation of knowledge from instruments claims that our justified belief in the reliability of an instrument consists of a few factors. To start, the agent must have a level of knowledge concerning the construction of an instrument. Furthermore, the agent must have good reasons to believe that the instrument is working according to its construction. For models, Simon (2010, Dahl) characterises this as the _transparency_ of a system and in order for a system to be transparent – it cannot be opaque.
