The classification of DNNs as a scientific instrument is a common one given the role they play within the unfolding of scientific discovery (Duede, Alvarado, find more). It’s important to note that DNNs are not just _similar_ to instruments (as some suggest e.g., Boge 2021). The contexts of their use within scientific understanding makes them qualitatively different than, for example, experiment or theory (Alvarado BOOK). In accepting this classification, certain epistemic commitments must follow - the ways in which we trust, rely, or justify DNNs must be the same for other scientific instruments. We can use the historical examples and results of instrument epistemology to inform our current treatment.

To begin, we need to try and arrive at a definition of a scientific instrument. Kletzl (CITE) proposes that the category, as a whole, is an intuitive one. Even current classifications of instruments do not ground themselves on a credible epistemic principle that justifies the collection of such different tools under the singular header of a ‘scientific instrument’. For example, there is still no widely accepted rationale for placing both the microscope and the large hadron collider in the same category. Attempts to remedy this have split the concept into multiple subcategories. 

The first of these, in the 17th century, divided the label into “philosophical” and “mathematical” instruments (Taub, 2011, SS pg.73). The former concerned itself with “pure truths” about nature’s inner working, employed mainly by “natural philosophers” and ranged from including tools such as telescopes and air pumps (Cite Kletzl) (Warner 1990). In essence, these philosophical instruments were concerned with the demonstration of a hypothesis through the usage of a particular object. In comparison, “mathematical instruments” were tools associated with measurement and manipulation of constructs, such as the quadrant. Historically, the division between both was not founded on any foundational epistemic rule, instead the split grew out of certain social, such as commercial, interests (Taub, 2009).

The lack of a guiding epistemic principle for what makes an object into an instrument means that any further sub-classification was discretionary. The consequence being that that some instruments can be argued to occupy both categories of mathematical and philosophical (van Helden 1983, 65). Despite this arbitrary labelling, other, more contemporary, systems of instrument taxonomy continue to draw divisions around similar lines.

Harre (2010, Equipment for an experiment) distinguishes between instruments and apparatus. The former is causally related to the world as tools which reveal natural phenomena. For example, a litmus solution is able to reveal whether a particular mixture is acidic or base. The instrument works through _exploiting_ a causal connection with the world. Within this category are most measuring devices – e.g., rulers, thermometers, and clocks. The thermometer is widely considered the characteristic epistemological example of a scientific instrument (Citation within Kletzl “Russel”) and, like all instruments of this class, its usefulness is dependent on its ability to respond to certain changes within the world to produce a resulting phenomenon. In this case of a mercury thermometer, the mercury will expand as the temperature of its surroundings increase.

In comparison, an apparatus is a tool which stands as an abstract model of some part of the world. In attempting to study the behaviour of ocean waves under controlled circumstances, we employ a specialised water tank constructed to model the behaviour of the ocean in which the conditions within the water tank are analogous to the conditions of the ocean. While there is no causal connection between the two, knowledge is produced, and is dependent upon, how well our model represents its related phenomena (Harre 2010).

Baird (2004, Thing Knowledge) draws a similar distinction between three types of scientific instruments: models; devices which create phenomenon; and measuring instruments. Models are similar to Harre’s apparatus but contains both mechanical and mathematical models and structures both objects and their relations (pg. 23). Therefore, for Baird, most models are conceptual instead of material. For example, Mathematics models specify relations between objects through equations, such as the law of gravitation F=Gr2m1​m2​​, which relates mass and distance to the force of attraction. This broader conception of a scientific instrument will be useful in our later treatment of DNNs as most statistical methods would be considered instruments.

Other systems of classification, such as Heidelbergers (CITE) match Baird in allowing mathematical models the status of instruments. He delineates four different roles a scientific instrument can play in the context of an experiment: Instruments that produce or split phenomena; modulating the behaviour of phenomena; imitating phenomena; and representing phenomena.

What is the purpose of this exercise? Within the differing taxonomies, we can identify certain static features of scientific instruments, along with two broad categories. Shared between every classification is a distinction between instruments that constructed in order to pick up causal relationships within the world (_causal instruments)_, and instruments that are constructed in order to model the world (_model instruments)_. Harré’s apparatuses, Baird’s measuring instruments and phenomenon-producing devices, along with Heidelberger’s instruments that generate, split, or modulate phenomena, all broadly invoke the idea that these instruments causally detect specific relationships within the world (Hacking 1983, Representing and intervening). 

In the case of _causal instruments_, the instrument takes advantage of an established causal pathway. Modern electromagnetic motors, for example, operate on a theoretical principle that a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force, as expressed by the Lorentz force law. When an electric current passes through the motor’s coil, the interaction between the generated magnetic field of the coil and the permanent (or electromagnetically induced) magnetic field of the stator produces torque, causing rotational motion (CITE).

While these principles are well understood and established now, Michael Faraday, at the time of his original experiments on electromagnetism in 1821, was unaware of these guiding laws. His construction of the electric motor was based on his theorised, and underdeveloped understanding, of electromagnetic laws. The final goal, of showing how a certain arrangement of electric and magnetic elements could produce motion, supported his theory by matching his hypothesized results (Faraday 1844, Thing Knowledge). Compared to contemporary motors, his device worked in a simpler fashion, in which a wire is suspended around a magnet allowing for the intersecting magnetic and electric fields to produce motion. The specific construction of the instrument depended on his working knowledge and the specific dynamics of the experiment. Therefore, the form an instrument takes arises out of the backdrop of theory and experiment.

Despite the lack of theoretical knowledge surrounding the behaviour of Faraday’s electromagnetic motor, the device was able to produce motion. Faraday did not have epistemic access to the underlying laws mediating the behaviour of his motor. He had a primitive theory of electromagnetism and constructed his motor around some posited causal connections. Any explanatory power came out of the instruments position within his greater theory. Strictly speaking, all the instrument did was produce motion. Explaining exactly why requires grounding in a certain theory – not necessarily a correct theory – but some sort of theory regardless.

_Model instruments_ arise out of the same logic. However, their utility is qualitatively different as it depends on a representative function. As stated before, our treatment of these instruments follows from Baird (2004) in including mathematical formulas, such as statistical techniques or experimental methods (e.g. Randomised Control Trials). While there are both material models (Watson and Cricks “ball and stick” DNA is an example) and conceptual models. Our discussion will mainly focus on the latter. By and large, ‘a model is an interpretative description of a phenomenon that facilitates access to that phenomenon (Bailer-Jones, 2009, p.1). Therefore, models are instruments which represent their target-phenomenon and facilitate the creation of explanatory theories regarding said phenomenon (Frigg & Hartmann 2020, Weisberg 2013 – analogue model citations).

For example, if we return to our example of Faraday, the mechanics of his electromagnetic motor were empirically recorded and produced a set of mathematical models. Maxwell reformulated Faraday’s observations into a formula expressing the law of induction, expressed as such:

\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}

(Faraday, Experimental Researches in Electricity, 1839)

The model was not constructed in isolation, but in response to a set of experimental outputs. These models, now known as Maxwell’s equations, are a type of _model instrument_ that both explains and predicts phenomena (Maxwell, A Treatise on Electricity and Magnetism, 1873).

To recap: 
Both causal and model instruments arise out of experimental contexts and depend on a level of theory to provide explanation. 

Returning to DNNs, what type of instrument are they? Generally they are considered to be _model instruments_ but is this entirely true? If _causal instruments_ pick up latent paterns within the world, then don’t DNNs – by picking up on a certain function – do exactly this? For example, a DNN trained on radiology images directly “responds” to the presence of a tumour by activating specific feature detectors.

What are models really? Can a DNN be a model?

Main Point: DNNs are statistical algorithms like any other. If a model needs to be explanatory relative to a human agent, then DNNs only sin is their lack of meaningful conceptual content. The difference between an opaque and a non-opaque model is the level of semantic and abstract causal clarity embedded within the model architecture itself. Why exactly are certain models opaque and others are not? Because relative to an agent, certain models can be traced through in a higher-level way, we can draw boundaries and say “this is doing X” and “that is doing Y”, however these boundaries are always ex post facto when applied to DNNs.

The architecture of DNNs are non-conceptual in nature. Opacity depends on this conceptuality.

Models are always created with this conceptual clarity in mind. The architecture emerges out of a certain explanatory framework. DNNs also follow in this, their original purpose was to mimic biological neurons and their process of pattern recognition. In this sense, they are perfect models. However, the success has given rise to applications far away from their original use.

This is where the confusion arises. Viewed in conjunction with their original goal of mimicking the human brain, the DNN provides ample explanatory content. This is because there is an isomorphic relation between the instrument and the phenomena. The model and phenomenon being studies are analogous because they were designed to be.

Like any scientific instrument that is employed in a different way for which it was originally constructed. There are doubts to whether it is acting as intended. The remedy to this is increasing the robustness of the instrument.
