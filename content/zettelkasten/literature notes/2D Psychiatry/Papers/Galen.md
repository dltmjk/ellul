Galen's humours was based on the paradigm of the four element. As such, any causal diagnosis had to be derived from this basis. Similarly, our currently medical model depends upon the foundations of germ theory. 

For psychiatric classifications both the A and C extension are variable. Whereras for other things, they are not variable, at least one of them is. The thing that is fixed is the relation to society and abject weirdness. Not to the actual symptomatic profile, like TB is. Therefore, we can see that the concept of a psychiatric disorder differs greatly from other somatic disorders. Because of the changing A and C intention. 

===Mention TB but don't talk about how Galen classified it===

Let us consider an alternate world in which Hippocrates was correct and the body actually is dictated by four different humours. 

The A-intention allows for reference fixing of a disease onto a set of symptoms. Symptoms allow us this reference fixing capability onto a certain object. In this case, either one of the humours or a certain bacteria. Conceptual change then, for example historical, will update the C-intention of a concept, but not the A-intention. 

As shown before, both Hippocrates and modern doctors noted the relevant symptoms for a TB diagnosis. However, their causal diagnosis differed. 



Psychiatric classifications, even if they do not refer to natural kinds, still can capture certain relations inherent within concepts. It cannot, however, tell us whether there is any such thing as as OCD (Jackson 1998a).
