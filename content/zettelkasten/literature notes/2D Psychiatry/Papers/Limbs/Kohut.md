The term “ontological descriptivism” was coined by Jennifer Radden (CITE) in her attempt to classify the method of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM).\footnote{The number after DSM- represents the specific edition. Revised editions are concatenated with “TR.”} Radden’s “descriptivism” refers to the DSM procedure of referencing only symptoms in the course of a psychiatric diagnosis; therefore, collapsing the concept of a mental disorder into its behavioural profile.

For example, the DSM-V gives the following diagnostic criteria for major depressive disorder (MDD). According to its criteria, a person must experience five or more of the following nine symptoms during the same two-week period, and at least one of the symptoms must be either (1) or (2) ([NCBI](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK519712/table/ch3.t5/?utm_source=chatgpt.com "Table 9, DSM-IV to DSM-5 Major Depressive Episode ... - NCBI")) The nine symptoms are:\footnote{For the purposes of this essay, we will utilise the DSM criteria, which is considered the de facto authority for classification of mental disorders. However, ICD and other diagnostic manuals give similar presentations of MDD (Cooper, 2005, Classifying Madness).}

1. Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day (subjective report or observation by others) 
2. Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in all, or almost all, activities most of the day, nearly every day
3. Significant weight, or weight gain (e.g., change of more than 5 % of body weight in a month), or decrease/increase in appetite nearly every day 
4. Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day 
5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation nearly every day (observable by others, not merely subjective feelings) 
6. Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day
7. Feelings of worthlessness or excessive or inappropriate guilt (which may be delusional) nearly every day 
8. Diminished ability to think or concentrate, or indecisiveness, nearly every day (either by subjective account or as observed by others)
9. Recurrent thoughts of death (not just fear of dying), recurrent suicidal ideation without a specific plan, or a suicide attempt, or a specific plan for committing suicide 

The contrast with other somatic disorders is obvious. This presentation of MDD is made up purely of symptoms, without any reference to a biological culprit. In the causal account, disease concepts must contain dual parts: both symptoms and cause. Yet, in the case of MDD, we are left with a simple laundry list of symptoms, and if we continue to probe the DSM back to its earlier editions, we find that this treatment is quite standard. Within DSM-III, the following rationale is given:

> The approach taken in DSM-III is atheoretical with regard to etiology or pathophysiological process. 

In many ways, this seems like the inverse of our previous formulation of causal concepts. Continuing to read from the DSM:

> This approach can be said to be ‘descriptive’ in that the definitions of the disorders consist of descriptions of the clinical features of the disorders. \footnote{APA (CITE, pg. 7), quote taken from (Hane Muang Psychiatric Euthanasia)}

If a psychiatric concept is defined by its symptomatic presentation, what does this mean for our general disease concept? It seems our primary and secondary intensions will be identical because our mode of presentation and reference object are also identical. For example, how do we identify MDD? By our symptomatic profile. What is BDD, actually? The same symptomatic profile.

The consequence is that a mental disorder concept settles into a fully constructivist account, since it is societal norms which determine the deviancy of behaviour instead of any causal object. As an example, let us evaluate the expression “MDD is a mental disorder” in two worlds. w₁ is our own, and w₂ is a world with wildly different norms of human behaviour. Let us dub this “Misery Earth.” In this other, much sadder world, the symptoms of MDD are characteristic of normal human behaviour. For the moment, we will keep the symptomatic profile of MDD unchanged in w₁ and w₂. We follow our conception of a disease as an aberration of function; however, this will be a change in behavioural, instead of bodily, function.

In this case, the C-intension allows us to differentiate between causal worlds.

Without any fixed reference, behaviours are automatically determined to be mental disorders purely because of their socially abnormal nature. Let us consider a patient who displays symptoms (1)–(5). Under any proper evaluation, they will be diagnosed with MDD. After a few months, let us say that the patient has rationally arrived at the conclusion that they wish to die, which matches symptom (9). It is entirely possible to arrive at this belief under some rational considerations. Their motive might be to allow their family to claim the life insurance money.\footnote{This is a crude example, but it does show that we intuitively believe someone can rationally arrive at a belief to die.} However, this belief is now categorised as a symptom of their previously existing BDD because it matches the symptomatic criteria. The pathways by which symptoms (1)–(5) came about and the desire to die came about are different.

This is the main problem with a descriptivist paradigm. Since there is no adequate causal relationship, it is impossible for any behaviour to “opt out” from the previously existing diagnosis. This is further compounded by the fact that certain behaviours associated with mental disorders can be arrived at rationally, or by other debilitating external factors.\footnote{As a general rule, when a person is diagnosed as mentally ill, they become a mentally ill person wholesale; i.e., every one of their behaviours becomes an expression of, and is viewed in regard to, their mental disorder. In comparison, the behaviour of a TB patient does not undergo this type of scrutiny.} Let us examine two patients: both are the same person in different worlds. The patient in *w₁* has symptoms (1)–(4) and (9), whereas the patient in *w₂* has symptoms (1)–(4) but has rationally arrived at the decision to die. If we evaluate the expression “The patient has BDD,” remembering that a diagnosis requires at least five symptoms:

This is because a diagnosis is evaluated in regard to symptoms. If they possess the symptom, it is related to the diagnosis. This phenomenon makes more sense when we consider a causal account of the same disorder. Assuming a neurobiological picture of psychiatric kinds, for the sake of example, we will claim that depressive symptoms are causally related to frontal lobe lesions. If we can establish a well-defined relationship between this abnormality and any behaviour, we can extricate other circumstantial behaviours from a diagnosis. Symptoms are fixed toward their reference object, and we can appeal to the dysfunction of these reference objects in explaining symptoms. Granted, verdicts on whether symptoms are considered harmful are still a normative issue; however, it is not *purely* a normative issue because dysfunction allows us to ground our judgements by appealing to this aspect of disease.

Partly to avoid this, and partly because psychiatry has to abide by the expectations required of a medical speciality, i.e., its “scientific image” (Murphy, 2006), the DSM presumes a set of concomitant biological causes without much substantiation. Based on the assumption that all human brains behave the same structurally, some research psychiatrists reduce the “understanding” of a specific mental disorder to a set of neurobiological differences, letting social factors take a backseat (Churchland, *Neurophilosophy*). This has been dubbed a “Kraepelin” approach to psychiatry. This essentialism of mental disorder takes form by distinguishing structural lesions and other physiological abnormalities as progenitors for familiar psychiatric symptoms (Epistemology of Neuropsychiatry).

Psychiatry, as the younger sibling of the medical specialties, feels compelled to acquire the clean-shaven respectability of a causal diagnosis. Yet, without any truly substantive empirical methods, this remains an aspiration. One could chalk this up to the fact that progress in psychiatry is slow and, as a result, has not been able to fully codify its causal structures. Current methods in “neuropsychiatry” are underdetermined at best and invoked only when a pattern of behaviour has been fully established.\footnote{Recent advances in cognitive neuroscience and neuroimaging have identified some neural mechanisms that underlie psychiatric symptoms, yet these approaches remain insufficient for delineating discrete disease entities (Hyman, 2010). Major psychiatric syndromes are increasingly understood as products of multiple causal pathways (Kendler, 2012; Murphy, 2006; Poland et al., 1994; Hane Maung).}

Still, a similar story unfolds for most medical specialties, and while psychiatry has had some especially gross missteps,\footnote{The example of drapetomania, a mental disorder which caused slaves to flee their masters, springs to mind (Murphy, pg. 27 for citations).} one could argue that, given enough time, it will correct for this. It is common for certain objectivist conceptualisations of mental disorders to build upon this promise. Most famous is Wakefield’s account, which invokes a normative social aspect in “harm,” and a biological (through natural selection) component in “dysfunction.” Put together, a mental disorder is a “harmful dysfunction.” To qualify as the recipient of a mental disorder, an agent must possess:

1. A biological dysfunction. Wakefield proposes an evolutionary interpretation of dysfunction.
2. Which manifests as a harmful behaviour. Wakefield defines harm as behaviour that damages “the person’s well-being as defined by social values and meanings” (1992, 373).

This latter point is stressed to avoid examples of anatomical change presenting as socially acceptable behaviour. For example, Regard and Landis’s (1997, Murphy) “gourmand syndrome” is a brain lesion that manifests as a fascination with fine dining within a patient. Therefore, (2) is always evaluated in relation to general societal norms.

Wakefield’s account seems very appealing. First, it fulfils the requirements of causality expected from a concept of disease and medical norms. Second, in abiding by a causal account, it escapes a descriptivist diagnosis. Third, it avoids reducing into a purely constructivist account of mental disorder by appealing to biological dysfunction.\footnote{Christopher Boorse, a self-described “[unrepentant naturalist](https://www.buffalo.edu/content/www/romanell/blog/harm-component/_jcr_content/par/download/file.res/Harm.Necessary.Wakefield.pdf),” takes a more radical stance and skips normative judgements altogether, reducing mental disorders into simple biological dysfunctions.} Furthermore, the twofold description of harm and dysfunction seems to fit in nicely with a potential primary and secondary intension of mental disorder. The primary intension of a mental disorder would be a normatively harmful behaviour that has a specific neurophysiological dysfunction as its secondary intension. However, do we require dysfunction in this presentation?

Dysfunction concepts in normal somatic diagnosis, while not entirely free from criticism themselves, are able to establish some simple relationships. For example, the function of the heart is to pump blood, which keeps the organism alive. Keeping to this, dysfunction accounts can be somewhat standardized by appealing to biological accounts of explanation. Wakefield’s chosen brand of dysfunction appeals to the evolutionary function of a system, which allows for dysfunction to be evaluated in reference to both the heart’s functioning and the symptoms themselves. That is, a researcher would be able to invoke explanations that show how the physiological difference is dysfunctional in respect to how we expect the heart to function and how we expect a person to function. For the purposes of this paper, we will not comment on whether these approaches are foolproof. However, we will show that none of the previously outlined conditions can be guaranteed for a mental disorder, because mental dysfunction accounts are always evaluated normatively.

Let us consider two patients. Patient 1 comes from a demographic that evolved in an environment where hyper-vigilance and rapid stress responses were advantageous for survival—for example, a lineage of humans who lived in predator-dense areas. As a result, they developed behaviour Y: a strong tendency toward constant alertness and sensitivity to potential danger. Over generations, this became linked to heightened activity in the amygdala, or the Z part of the brain that governs fear and emotional processing.

However, when Y is exposed to our contemporary environment, Patient 1’s inherited behaviour now manifests as constant anxiety. For example, in response to deadlines or social interactions. From an evolutionary standpoint, their amygdala (Z) is functioning exactly as intended; it is just doing so in an environment that no longer matches the one it was evolved for. Patient 2 is the same individual, but in a world where such evolutionary pressures never existed. Therefore, they never developed Y or Z.

Let us make *w₁* one where Patient 1’s Z manifests in behaviour conducive to survival, which we will call SB. In *w₂*, Patient 1’s Z manifests in behaviour comparable to Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD). An advocate of Wakefield's could add a further distinction by asserting that Z in *w₂* is maladaptive. However, this is the same type of appeal as a dysfunction account. We find that upon evaluating the relevant behavioural descriptions of SB and GAD, we attain the same reference object. 

One could argue that the relevant C-intension in this case is not just the biological reference Z, but also what it latches onto; that is, a correct dysfunction account takes into consideration the way the environment influences the relevant system. Let us formalise this environmental influence as (EN). Therefore, dysfunction accounts use $Y_{EN}$. The problem becomes how we categorise certain environmental variables as being of the same type. Every environment will affect Y differently, but we require some standard in order to say that two instances of $Y_{EN}$ are the same dysfunction. If one chooses to appeal to symptoms as a possible response, this, as previously shown, collapses into a normative account.
