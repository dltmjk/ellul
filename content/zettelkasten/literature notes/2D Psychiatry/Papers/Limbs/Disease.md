A concept of disease escapes the neat and tidy linguistic analysis that philosophers have come to prefer (Reznek, 1987)\footnote{For example, "X is a disease if and only if Y, and X is polio if and only if Z."}. Multiple alternatives have been proposed, given the concept’s moral and social importance\footnote{See Reiss & Ankeny (2016)}, the most promising and intuitively appealing candidate being that of causal structures (Thagard, 1997; Medin, 1985, 1989). Explanation, in this form, traces symptomatic profiles to a likely cause. When someone claims that agent X is afflicted with disease Y, they are not claiming that X presents a particular set of behaviours and that Y is just the presentation of these symptoms. Instead, the belief is that X possesses a certain affliction (e.g., in the form of an anatomical difference or pathogen) that produces the relevant symptoms. Causal structures require an etiological account of these symptoms in order to be considered a valid disease concept.

This seems to provide an intuitive epistemological basis for both the explanation and conceptualisation of disease. Even without contemporary scientific know-how, ancient medical practitioners practised and theorised about illness along similar lines. Hippocrates provided his account by tracing disease to a malfunction of the four “humours.”

> All human diseases arise from bile and phlegm; the bile and phlegm produce diseases when, inside the body, one of them becomes too moist, too dry, too hot, or too cold; they become this way from foods and drinks, from exertions and wounds, from smell, sound, sight, and venery, and from heat and cold. (Hippocrates, 1988, p. 7).

A disease, therefore, was spurred on by a specific part not fulfilling its intended purpose, either through excess or deficiency.\footnote{This pattern of thinking reflects the selected effects account of dysfunction (Cite Selected Effects Paper)} (https://catalogue.nla.gov.au/catalog/997169). 

This treatment of disease might appear redundant, given that it seems to echo the well-established logic of cause and effect. Our choice gains some respectability when evaluated in reference to other commonly employed theories of conceptual analysis: either a classical or a prototype theory, both of which offer limited applicability to a disease concept. The classical theory, for example, would standardise a concept of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) into a collection of symptoms: $RA = \set{\text{inflammation}, \text{stiffness}, \text{painful joints}}$. While this generally captures one sense of what RA is, it leaves open the possibility of two separate diseases, with differing treatments, having the exact same set of symptoms. Another major difficulty is deciding which symptoms even deserve membership to begin with (Sadegh-Zadeh, K. The Prototype Resemblance Theory of Disease).

Given this, what does a causal conception of disease actually look like? We will use the example of tuberculosis (TB). Hippocrates gives the following symptomatic profile: “constant sweats, not diffused over the whole body” and a “protracted diurnal fever” ([\cite](https://sci-hub.se/10.5588/ijtld.14.0736)). Modern clinicians, by and large, follow a similar diagnostic profile in order to diagnose TB. While Hippocrates appealed to the four humours, our current medical model depends upon the results of a mature germ theory. According to Thagard (1997), disease conceptualisation proceeds in a few steps:

(1) Distinctive behaviours are collated together into a symptom profile.
(2) Certain causes are nominated as origins for the symptom profile depending on the current medical paradigm.
(3) This causal hypothesis is tested through experimentation or other relevant evidence/methods in order to produce good reason for the proposed aetiology.

Still, a causal account leaves one important question unanswered: what qualifies as a set of distinctive behaviours? Or, more generally, what makes something a disease instead of, for example, an injury? While a well-defined concept of disease in the literature has yet to be arrived at, the most popular are naturalistic or “dysfunction” accounts, which characterise illness as the malfunctioning of biological parts (Norman Daniels, 2007). For the purposes of this essay, we will adopt a general position and say that disease is a dysfunction of normal human systems (Norman Daniels, 1985). Whether the specifics of a disease concept are well defined or not does not change our conclusion and general argumentation.\footnote{Our essay seeks to show how such dysfunction accounts are superfluous when applied to mental disorders. Therefore, making our treatment of it as general as possible serves a rhetorical purpose later when we mount arguments against it.}

Our current brand of explanation is microbial.\footnote{For the sake of sketching a toy case, we will restrain our analysis to microbacterial causes. Later, we will introduce other disease types.} Therefore, any aetiology of TB requires a sketch of the suspect microorganism, namely the microbe Mycobacterium tuberculosis (which we will shorten to just “mycobacterium”). The question needs to be raised: if medical knowledge has changed considerably from Hippocrates to today, have our concepts of disease remained unchanged?

Structurally, both Hippocrates and modern medicine appeal to the same causal structure, yet the particulars of this explanation are worlds apart. One could argue that the two formulations of TB compose two separate concepts because the former Hippocratic approach can reduce TB to a phlegm imbalance and the latter to a bacterial presence.\footnote{This manoeuvre commits us to a certain essentialism or naturalism about bodily diseases. For our current treatment of bacterial disorders, this decision is fairly uncontroversial.} If the concept of TB, or any general disease, is synonymous with its relevant cause, are these two concepts in conflict? And if they are the same concept, how can we account for this change?

To help in answering this, we can employ the methods of two-dimensional semantics (or “2D semantics”). Originally formulated by Stalnaker (1978) and extended greatly by Jackson (1998), it provides a framework for analysing meaning and reference across possible worlds.

In short, a primary or “A”(ctual)-intension\footnote{Different terminology is employed in the presentation and usage of 2D semantics. For the purposes of this essay, we will use both primary/secondary and A/C intensions interchangeably.} is a function for an expression (or term) that maps it to an extension in every actual world. Formally, ( f_A : W_{a} \to Ext ). An important aspect is that the A-intension represents how speakers in the world would use the expression, allowing it to act as a reference-fixing criterion. In the example of water, this might take the form of “substances of the same basic nature as the watery stuff of our acquaintance,” which has the extension H₂O in our world ([DM](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9781137344557_2)).

A secondary or “C”(ounterfactual)-intension of an expression is a function that maps it to its extension in every counterfactual world. Formally, ( f_C : W_{c} \to Ext ) (Jackson, 1998a, ch. 2). For example, the C-intension of the term “water” will pick out H₂O in the actual world, and in a counterfactual world in which the same substance exists under different conditions, it will still pick out H₂O. An expression’s 2D-intension assigns it a secondary intension from any actual world: ( f_{2D} : W_{a} \times W_{c} \to Ext ).

To illustrate the use of both the 2D method and primary/secondary intensions, we can use the commonly cited example of Putnam’s (1975) “Twin Earth,” where a liquid with the superficial properties and mode of presentation of water is composed not of H₂O but of XYZ. Let $w_1$ and w₂ represent our world and Twin Earth, respectively. The vertical axis represents the worlds that we will consider as actual, whereas the horizontal axis represents the counterfactual worlds. The diagonal in the following tables will be our A-intension, and the horizontal will be our C-intension. As an example, we will evaluate the expression “water is H₂O,” which produces the following table:

.
    w1, w2
w1   T F
w2   F F
CAPTION EVERY TABLE WITH THE EXPRESSION (Expression: Water is H20)

Let us extend this to a concept of disease by considering the example of TB in two possible worlds. The first is our own, designated w₁, in which TB is traceable to mycobacterium (MB). Let w₂ be a counterfactual world in which the body is governed by four distinct humours and TB fulfils the Hippocratic causal account.\footnote{If the example of the humours seems unintuitive, you could substitute it with another similar bacterium that is not mycobacterium, since it is entirely, and intuitively, possible for what we call TB to be caused by this parallel organism.} For the sake of presentation, we will say that TB in w₂ is an increase in phlegm (P).\footnote{We are also assuming that they have a method to experimentally confirm this presence.} We will evaluate two expressions.

The first, “TB is what presents S,” where S is the symptomatic profile of TB, establishes the synchronicity of the A-intension with the symptomatic profile. Our table is as follows:

.
	w1, w2
w1     T     T  
w2     T     T


In both $w_1$ and *$w_2$, the relevant symptoms are the same: fever, coughing, sweats, etc.; therefore, evaluated at every world, the extension is true. We have already said that the A-intension roughly corresponds to the pre-empirical presentation of the object in question, before any *a posteriori* fact is discovered. Therefore, for any disease concept, this would be the symptomatic profile. Let us now evaluate the expression “TB is what actually causes S”:

.
    w₁   w₂
w₁       T    F
w₂       F    T


In this case, the concept of TB is fixed to the actual world’s causal culprit, either MB or P, which is false when evaluated at the opposing counterfactual worlds. In the previous two examples, we can see that the A-intension is the same, but the C-intension differs. Therefore, we can claim that the conceptual change between the Hippocratic and biomedical causal accounts of TB is due to a changing C-intension.\footnote{It may be controversial to perform this operation. Our previous account contrasts $w_1$ and *$w_2$, whereas, if we say that the C-intension changes in our world, it would be a difference between *time₁* and *time₂*. The legitimacy of this procedure is questionable, but our general point stands: historical progressions of a disease concept differ in relation to the reference object. This point is made by Carey (1992) and Thagard, who characterise this as a relational change between two properties.} This integrates neatly with our previous causal account, where the causal object (C-intension) and the produced symptoms (A-intension) are two ends of a disease concept.\footnote{This approach also shares similarities with Kitcher’s (1993) view of referential change, which described three *modes of reference*: descriptive, which allows an agent to pick out an object while employing a description; baptismal, for when an agent uses an ostensive mechanism to apply a term to an object; and conformist, which takes advantage of another’s established reference. Using this, we can say that TB underwent a baptismal, but not a descriptive, change.}

There are some concerns that may arise with our presentation of a disease concept:

(1) If a disease concept has this dual constitution, why do clinicians treat purely in respects to the causal variable? There must be a sense in which a disease supervenes into its C-intension

Let us return to our example of water. Chemical analysis allows us to fix an extension of water, but it does not play a meaningful part in our everyday treatment of water. Clinicians approach disease similarly. Symptoms act as a mode of presentation, which allows for a fixing of extension, but this does not constitute the entire meaning of a disease. Certain contextual uses of a concept must appeal to certain properties to fulfil the requirements of that context.

Lalumera (2014) advances this split between concepts and concepts that perform a certain role (Lalumera, 2014). For example, diabetes is classified as a dysfunction of pancreatic β-cells according to WHO.\footnote{A slightly contentious identification; discussion can be found in [[Are mental disorders brain disorders]]} However, this conception is engineered as the best fit for treatment and should not be taken as representing the entire concept of diabetes. The inverse case, of a clinician recognising diabetes, appeals only to symptoms. Yet it would not be right to assert that the concept of diabetes supervenes on this.

(2) Does the A-intention of a disease remain completely unchanged despite paradigm, or C-intention, changes? 

One could argue that, by updating the C-intension through a paradigm change, we are now able to re-categorize our understanding of previous symptoms, thereby classifying them as a different type. For example, germ theory afforded us greater explainability in tracing symptom relationships. While this may initially seem convincing, it mixes up the way in which a reference is fixed. If we return to water, it is entirely possible for us to discover that the liquid clear drinkable substance around us is actually XYZ instead of H₂O. If this were to happen, would this change anything about the mode of presentation of water?

Granted, it would give us greater understanding of the mechanisms and potential behaviour of this new ABC-water. However, these are changes in the conceptual vocabulary of an object, not of the A-intension.\footnote{This concern is prompted by Kuhn (1993), who argues that concept change prompted by paradigm change produces parallel concepts which are irreconcilable. Both will describe, and generalise, upon the world with separate and non-isomorphic vocabularies (see Buchwald, 1992).}

Previously, we assumed that disease was a purely infectious affair in our previous detailing of TB; however, diseases can also be causally related to a person's genetics, diet, immune function, body chemistry, and cell/anatomical structure. Our next section will focus on a type of disorder which is “mental” in nature and treated by psychiatric clinicians.
