A concept of disease escapes the neat and tidy linguistic analysis that philosophers have come to prefer (Reznek, 1987) \footnote{For example "X is a disease if and only if Y, and X is consumption if and only if Z}. Multiple alternatives have been proposed, the most promising, and intuitively appealing, candidate being that of causal structures (Thagard 1997, Medin 1985, 1989). Explanation, in this form, traces symptomatic profiles to a respective cause. Contemporary clinical practice employs diagnostic labels in order to refer to the underlying cause of symptom profiles. When someone claims that agent x is inflicted with disease, they are not claiming that X presents a particular set of behaviours, broadly in line with the expected symptoms. Instead, the belief is that X possess a certain affliction (e.g., in the form of a anatomical difference or pathogen) which produces the relevant symptoms. Causal structures require an etiological account of these symptoms in order to be considered a valid disease concept. 

This seems to provide an intuitive epistemological basis for both the explanation and conceptualisation of diseases. Even without contemporary scientific know-how, ancient medical practitioners practised and theorised on illness along similar lines. Hippocrates, provided his account by reducing disease to a malfunction of the four "humours"; blood, phlegm, and black or yellow bile. If they existed in equilibrium, the human body maintained its good health. A disease, therefore, was spurred on by a specific part not fulfilling its intended purpose, either through either excess or deficiency \footnote{As we will explore later, this pattern of thinking reflects the selected effects account of disease} (https://catalogue.nla.gov.au/catalog/997169). 

Our treatment of disease might appear derivative. Our choice gains a greater respectability when viewed in respect to other commonly employed theories of conceptual analysis. For example, most common are either classical to prototype theories, both of which offer limited applicability. Classical theory would standardise a concept of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) into a collection of symptoms; $RA = \set{\text{inflammation}, \text{stiffness}, \text{painful joints}}$. While this generally captures one sense of what RA is, it leaves open the possibility of two separate diseases, with differing treatments, having the exact same set of symptoms. Another major difficulty is deciding which symptoms even deserve membership to begin with (Sadegh-Zadeh K. The prototype resemblance theory of disease). 

Given this, what does a causal conception of disease actually look like? We'll use the example of Tuberculosis (TB). Hippocrates gives the following symptomatic profile: 'constant sweats, not diffused over the whole body' and a 'protracted diurnal fever' ([\cite](https://sci-hub.se/10.5588/ijtld.14.0736)). Modern clinicians, by and large, follow a similar diagnostic profile in order to diagnose TB. While Galen appealed to the four humours, our current medical model depends upon the results of a mature germ theory. According to Thagard, disease conceptualisation proceeds in a few steps. First, these symptoms are collated together into a distinctive profile. Second, certain causes are nominated depending on the specific profile and current medical paradigm. Third, the hypothesis is tested through experimentation or other relevant evidence in order to produce good reason for the proposed aetiology. 

Our current brand of explanation is microbiological, jetstarted by discoveries in fermentation. Any aetiology of TB requires a sketch of the suspect microorganism, namely the bacterium 'Mycobacterium'. What is the point of the exercise? First, it is obvious that medical knowledge has evolved from Hippocrates to today. Second, due to this progress, our understanding and treatment of TB has changed considerably. What does this mean for the concept of TB? Structurally, both Hippocrates and modern medicine appeal to the same causal structure, yet the particulars of this explanation are worlds apart. The question needs to be raised: is the concept of TB the same? and if it is, how has it changed?
