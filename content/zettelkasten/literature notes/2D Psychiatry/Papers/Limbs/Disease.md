Our purpose in this section is to establish the two 'senses' of a disease; the symptoms and aetiological object. Therefore, our treatment for both somatic and mental disorders is of conceptual analysis and not engineering. 

Our treatment of mental disorders is of conceptual analysis, not engineering. 

A concept of disease escapes the neat and tidy linguistic analysis that philosophers have come to prefer (Reznek, 1987) \footnote{For example "X is a disease if and only if Y, and X is polio if and only if Z}. Multiple alternatives have been proposed given the concepts moral and social importance \footnote{See Reiss & Ankeny (2016)}, the most promising, and intuitively appealing, candidate being that of causal structures (Thagard 1997, Medin 1985, 1989). Explanation, in this form, traces symptomatic profiles to a likely cause. When someone claims that agent X is inflicted with disease Y, they are not claiming that X presents a particular set of behaviours and Y is just the presentation of these symptoms. Instead, the belief is that X possess a certain affliction (e.g., in the form of a anatomical difference or pathogen) which produces the relevant symptoms. Causal structures require an etiological account of these symptoms in order to be considered a valid disease concept. 

A well defined causal account provides some distinct privileges. First, they yield projectable inferences (e.g. h20 will freeze at 0 degrees) ===interventionist thing===

Adding to this, a causal account yields projectable inferences, for example, the prediction that H20 will freeze below 0 celcius, because they put forward the necessary and sufficient properties of water (Goodman 1954; Quine 1969).


==Rundown of Causal Structure===

This seems to provide an intuitive epistemological basis for both the explanation and conceptualisation of diseases. Even without contemporary scientific know-how, ancient medical practitioners practised and theorised on illness along similar lines. Hippocrates, provided his account by tracing disease to a malfunction of the four "humours". 

> All human diseases arise from bile and phlegm; the bile and phlegm produce diseases when, inside the body, one of them becomes too moist, too dry, too hot, or too cold; they become this way from foods and drinks, from exertions and wounds, from smell, sound, sight, and venery, and from heat and cold. (Hippocrates, 1988, p. 7).

A disease, therefore, was spurred on by a specific part not fulfilling its intended purpose, either through either excess or deficiency \footnote{This pattern of thinking reflects the selected effects account of dysfunction (Cite Selected Effects Paper)} (https://catalogue.nla.gov.au/catalog/997169). 

This treatment of disease might appear especially redundant given that it might appear to echo the well established logic of cause and effect. Our choice gains some respectability when evaluated in reference to other commonly employed theories of conceptual analysis: either a classical or a prototype theory. Both of which offer limited applicability to a disease concept. Classical theory, for example, would standardise a concept of rheumatoid arthritis (RA) into a collection of symptoms; $RA = \set{\text{inflammation}, \text{stiffness}, \text{painful joints}}$. While this generally captures one sense of what RA is, it leaves open the possibility of two separate diseases, with differing treatments, having the exact same set of symptoms. Another major difficulty is deciding which symptoms even deserve membership to begin with (Sadegh-Zadeh K. The prototype resemblance theory of disease). 

Given this, what does a causal conception of disease actually look like? We'll use the example of Tuberculosis (TB). Hippocrates gives the following symptomatic profile: 'constant sweats, not diffused over the whole body' and a 'protracted diurnal fever' ([\cite](https://sci-hub.se/10.5588/ijtld.14.0736)). Modern clinicians, by and large, follow a similar diagnostic profile in order to diagnose TB. While Hippocrates appealed to the four humours, our current medical model depends upon the results of a mature germ theory. According to Thagard (1997), disease conceptualisation proceeds in a few steps: 

(1) Distinctive behaviours are collated together into a symptom profile. 
(2) Certain causes are nominated as origins for the symptom profile depending on the current medical paradigm. 
(3) This causal hypothesis is tested through experimentation or other relevant evidence/methods in order to produce good reason for the proposed aetiology. 

Still, a causal account leaves one important question unanswered; what qualifies as a set of distinctive behaviours? Or more generally, what makes something a disease instead of, for example, an injury? While a well-defined concept of disease in the literature remains elusive, most popular are naturalistic or 'dysfunction' accounts which characterise illness as the malfunctioning of biological parts (Norman Daniels 2007). For the purposes of this essay, we will adopt a general position and say that disease is an aberration of normal human functioning, since whether the specifics of a disease concept are well defined or not does not change our conclusion \foonote{Our essay seeks to show how such dysfunction accounts are contradictory when applied to mental disorders. Therefore, making our treatment of it as general as possible serves a rhetorical purpose later when we mount arguments against it.}. 

Our current brand of explanation is microbial \footnote{For the sake of sketching a toy case we will restrain our analysis to microbacterial causes. Later we will introduce other disease types.}. Therefore, any aetiology of TB requires a sketch of the suspect microorganism, namely the microbe 'Mycobacterium tuberculosis' (which we will shorten to just mycobacterium). The question needs to be raised: If medical knowledge has changed considerably from Hippocrates to today, have our concepts of disease remained unchanged? 

Structurally, both Hippocrates and modern medicine appeal to the same causal structure, yet the particulars of this explanation are worlds apart. One could argue that the two formulations of TB compose two separate concepts because the former Hippocratic approach can reduce TB to a phlegm imbalance and the latter to a bacterial presence \footnote{This manoeuvre commits us to a certain essentialism or naturalism about bodily diseases. For our current treatment of bacterial disorders, this decision is fairly uncontroversial.}. If the concept TB, or any general disease, is synonymous with its relevant cause, are these two concepts in conflict? And if they are the same concept, how can we account for this change? 

To help in answering this, we can employ the methods of Two-Dimensional semantics (or '2D' semantics). Originally formulated by Stalnaker (1978), and extended greatly by Jackson (1998), 

In short, a primary or 'A'(ctual)-intension \footnote{Different terminology is employed in the presentation and usage of 2D semantics. For the purposes of this essay, we will use both primary/secondary and A/C intensions interchangeably} is a function for an expression (or term) which maps it to an extension in every actual world. Formally, $f_A : W_{a} \to Ext$. An important aspect is that the A-intension represents how speaking in the world would use the expression, allowing for it to act as a reference fixing criterion. In the example of water, this might take the form of "substances of the same basic nature as the watery stuff or our acquaintance" which has the extension of H20 in our world ([DM](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9781137344557_2)). 

A secondary or 'C'(ounterfactual)-intension of an expression is a function which maps it to its extension in every counterfactual world. Formally, $f_C : W_{c} \to Ext$ (Jackson 1998a, ch.2). For example, the C-intension of the term “water” will pick out H₂O in the actual world, and in a counterfactual world in which the same substance exists under different conditions, it will still pick out H₂O. An expressions 2D-intension assigns it a secondary intention from any actual world $f_{2D} : W_{a} \times W_{c} \to Ext$. 

To illustrate the use of both the 2D method and primary/secondary intentions. We can use the commonly cited example of Putnam’s (1975) “Twin Earth,” where a liquid with the superficial properties, and mode of presentation, of water is composed not of H₂O but of XYZ. Let w1 and w2 represent our world and twin earth respectively. The vertical axis is the worlds which we will consider as actual, whereas the horizontal axis are the counterfactual worlds. The diagonal in these following tables will be our A-intension, and the horizontal is our C-intension. As an example, we will evaluate the expression "water is h20" which produces the following table: 

.
    w1, w2
w1   T F
w2   F F

Let's extend this to a concept of disease by considering the example of TB in two possible words. The first is our own, designated w1, in which TB is traceable to mycobacterium (MB). Let w2 be a counterfactual world in which the body is governed by four distinct humours and TB fulfils the Hippocratic causal account \footnote{If the example of the humours seems un-intuitive, you could substitute it with another similar bacterium that is not mycobacterium. Since it is entirely, and intuitively, possible for what we call TB to be caused by this parallel organism.}. For the sake of presentation, we’ll say that TB in w2 is an increase in phlegm (P) \footnote{We are also assuming that they have a method to experimentally confirm this presence}. We will evaluate two expressions. 

The first: "TB is what presents S" where S is the symptomatic profile of TB establishes the synchronicity of the A-intention with the symptomatic profile. Our table is as such: 

.
	w1, w2
w1     T     T  
w2     T     T

In both w1 and w2, the relevant symptoms are the same: fever, coughing, sweats, etc, therefore evaluated at every world the extension is true. We have already said that the A-intension roughly corresponds to the pre-empirical presentation of the object in question, before any a posteriori fact is discovered. Therefore, for any disease concept, this would be the symptomatic profile. Let's now evaluate the expression "TB is what actually causes S":

.
	w1, w2
w1    T  F
w2    F   T

In this case, the concept of TB is fixed to the actual worlds causal culprit; either MB or P. Which is false when evaluated at the opposing counterfactual worlds. In the previous two examples, we can see that the A-intention is the same but the C-intention differs. Therefore, the conceptual change between the Hippocratic and biomedical causal accounts of TB is due to a changing C-intension. This nicely integrates with our previous causal account where the causal object (C-intention), and the produced symptoms (A-intention) are two ends of a disease concept. \footnote{This approach shares similarities with Kitcher's (1993) view of referential change, which described three *modes of reference*; descriptive which allows for an agent to pick out a object while employing a description; baptismal for when an agent uses an ostensive mechanism to apply a term onto an object; and conformist which takes advantage of another's established reference. Using this, we can say that TB underwent a baptismal, but not a descriptive, change}

There are some concerns that may arise with our presentation of a disease concept:

(1) If a disease concept has this dual constitution, why do clinicians treat purely in respects to the causal variable? There must be a sense in which a disease supervenes into its C-intension

Let's return to our example of water. Chemical analysis allows us to fix an extenstion of water, but does not play a meaningful part in our everyday treatment of water. Clinicians approach disease similarly. Symptoms act as a mode of presentation which allows for a fixing of extension, but this does not constitute the entire meaning of a disease. Certain contextual uses of a concept must appeal to certain properties to fulfil the requirements of that context. 

Elisabetta Lalumera (2014) advances this split between concepts and concepts which perform a certain role (Lalumera, 2014). For example, diabetes is classified as a dysfunction of pancreatic β-cells according to WHO \footnote{A slightly contentious identification, discussion can be found in [[Are mental disorders brain disorders]]}. However, this conception is one engineered as the best fit for treatment and should not be taken as representing the entire concept of diabetes. The inverse case, of a clinician recognising diabetes, appeals only to symptoms. Yet it wouldn't be right to assert that the concept of diabetes supervenes into this. 

(2) Does the A-intention of a disease remain completely unchanged despite paradigm, or C-intention, changes? 

One could argue that, by updating the C-intension through a paradigm change, we are now able to re-categorize our understanding of previous symptoms, thereby classifying them as a different type. For example, germ theory afforded us greater explainability in tracing symptom relationships. While this may seem initially convincing, it mixes up the way in which a reference is fixed. If we return to water, it is entirely possible for us to discover that the liquid clear drinkable stuff around us is actually XYZ instead of H20. If this were to happen, would this change anything about the mode of presentation of water? 
Granted, it would give us greater understanding into the mechanisms and potential behaviour of this new ABC-water. However, these are changes in the conceptual vocabulary of an object, not of the A-intention \footnote{ This concern is prompted by Kuhn (1993). Who argues that concept change prompted by paradigm change produce parallel concepts which are irreconcilable. Both will describe, and generalise, upon the world with separate, and nonisomorphic, vocabularies (See Buchwald 1992).}.
