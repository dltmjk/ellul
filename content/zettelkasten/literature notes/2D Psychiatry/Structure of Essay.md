1. Preliminaries 

"When we are deciding whether some condition is a disease, we are in effect deciding what sort of people we ought to be" (Reznek, 1987, p. 166).

Thesis: 

What we group into the concept of a mental dysfunction and what we don't is often contradictory. Appealing to both neurobiological accounts and social ones without any consensus. Doctors wish to appeal to the former in order to give psychiatry the credence it deserves as a medical speciality. However, mental disorders are still justified and constructed on a socially normative basis.

By analysing this using a 2D account, we can back up our above claims.

The meaning of a psychiatric disorder is much more tied to its symptomatic profile than other disorders - you've already said this, and better, you need to put everything in a document and tie it together. 

Neurobiological effects without any corresponding symptoms do not seem to fulfil our expectations of a mental disorder. Furthermore, if you were to turn upto a certain clinic with this, you would be denied treatment as it needs to incur suffering in some way. Some will argue that doctors treat asymptomatic diseases because they have the possibility of causing harm later. However, the same considerations cannot be said for mental disorders. Take schizophrenia. While we have no real material method to biologically show that a person has schizophrenia. We have good reason to believe that a person possesses the disease if every one of their family members does. However, we do not conduct treatment in this case. 



Use 2D to show that symptoms, the 1-intention, is much more important for the meaning of the mental disorder, than the 2-intention. 

Can symptoms act as a causal account for a certain psychiatric disorder. The same way other somatic disorders do? 

Generally what we are looking for is a causal account. It is epistemically contentious to say that a person have depression because of x, which causes y. When x and y are the same. 

If we apply a 2-dimensional semantic framework, we can see that the 1-intention changes, but the 2-intention is supposed to stay the same. This is if we commit ourselves to a minimal objectivism about mental disorders. 
- Does a 2D semantic analysis only work if we commit ourselves to the position that mental disorders are natural kinds? What type of natural kinds? (Neurobiological, other?)

If we can continue by showing that psychiatric types are not natural kinds, then how does this affect a 2D semantic analysis? Are 2D semantic analysis must only be in reference to natural kinds? 

A disorder, if not a natural kind, gains its meaning in relation to the normative character of society. Something is only a disorder when it is undesirable given a certain form of life. 

Biological, and sociocultural definitions of disorder making up the 1 and 2-intention. Biological is when something does not fulfil its function. What you require is a biological function of what something is meant to do. This things are post-hoc explanations, always relative and applied by an agent. This type of confusion can be illustrated with perpetual disagreements of the function of the appendix. 

Therefore, mental disorders are socially normative and judgement varies greatly depending on the person you ask.
