1. The Dunzer story, within the Gopnik reading, showed that imagined characters could pose real world consequences. Despite children generally being aware that their imaginary friend is not real, and not apprehendable by other people, they still feel it has the potential to cause harm. Dunzer, for example, became "more and more hostile". In the Dadlez reading, the adults are startled after watching a horror movie and turning their lights off all at once. This is explained, in part, by the natural urge to place oneself in the shoes of the horror movie protagonist
   
   Similarly, when you watch a horror movie at night and try to go to sleep, there is a bit of fear that the events of the horror movie could occur to you. If we assume that the use of imaginary friends and parts of fiction is to provide counterfactuals for human behaviour, and if counterfactuals are used to inform future behaviour, then could this behaviour be explainable by mixing up the functional use of counterfactuals for the way the world could actually turn out? For example, by entertaining a certain scenario, do we now believe that that scenario has a chance of occurring? 

2. Furthermore, do we ever really separate the imaginary nature of counterfactuals from our sense of how the future could turn out, given how closely both of them inform each other? Does this explain certain irrational responses to unreal events? For example, upon reading a tragedy, there is a slight sense of paranoia that your own life is due for a tragic event, is this explainable only through identification of the events of the play, or do the previous considerations play a role?
