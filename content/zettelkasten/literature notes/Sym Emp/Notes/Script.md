Slide 1: Thesis, Theme
Argument: Representations serve as props in inviting us into a game of make-believe. The way that we are "brought into" this game requires formation of emotional attitudes and of a mental (simulationist) model that structures objects and can be used to explore counterfactuals. We argue that these models are the same ones that are employed as a child and in the exploration of future hypotheticals and that our emotional reaction towards fiction can be partly explained in the sharing of this structure.

Slide 3: Representation and Fiction, make-believe 
We will be drawing from Walton's work mimesis as make believe. Within this work, Walton uses the terms fiction and representation interchangeable. He gives the following definition for representation. 

Representations, I have said, are things possessing the social function of serving as props in games of make-believe, although they also prompt imaginings and are sometimes objects of them as well. A prop is something which, by virtue of conditional principles of generation, mandates imaginings. Propositions whose imaginings are mandated are fictional, and the fact that a given proposition is fictional is a fictional truth. 

(Walton, 1990 , p. 69)

Within this view, fiction is a prop that gives rise to a type of make-believe. In which make-believe is believing that one thing is another. For example, that text is treated as a record of events, situations, conversations in the same way that children pretend a pillow is a ghost. 

And to participate in a game of make-believe is to be the following: 

"Participants in games of make-believe are thus props, objects, and
imaginers all three, intimately combined in one neat package. They
prescribe imaginings-imaginings that are about themselves by virtue
of the fact that they themselves do the prescribing-and it is to them-
selves that they issue the prescriptions" (Walton, 1990, p. 224)

Representations ask something of us depending on the prop and its nature. Histories ask us to imagine life in another time. Pictures invite imagining in a sort of “indirect seeing”. The ways that we do this for fiction is the creation of a simulationist model. The type of representation also has a certain way of bringing us into the representation. For example, the way we engage with and, as we will explore later, model an artwork is different from that of fiction. Participating in a game of make-believe also necessitates a certain emotional involvement, due to participant perscribing imaginings-imaginings in relation to themselves. 

Fictional works then prescribe imaginings about their content, where imagining the content is participating in the game of make-believe authorized by the work. Games meaning that there are certain rules within this process of make-believe. For example, the fictional world permits certain causal relationships and restricts others. While we do not require a full continuous awareness of each rule, we are aware of what cannot be done. In modelling these relationships and behaviours we construct a type of mental model of the fictional work. 

Slide 3: Gopnik 

This is similar to the process of a childish imaginary friends. In Gopniks philosophical baby, we explore the construction and use of imaginary worlds for children. As we see, there seems to be a link between the functional use of fiction and of imaginary worlds. Gopnik points out : 

"Children who watched a lot of television were less likely to have imaginary companions, but that was also true of children who read a lot of books—children who were immersed in someone else’s imaginary world seemed less likely to create such a world themselves." pg 52 philosophical baby. 

These imaginary worlds are a type of make-believe. They are connected to certain props. For example, toys on a playground, but can have a life of their own. The rules of their make-believe are informed by real world experiences, which allow for the child to explore and learn. They learn what is permissible for the imaginary friend and what is not. The child also learns about what emotional reactions are expected from what social cues. They are integrated, and experiment with, a form of life. If our mental models start like this, then they require an emotional constituent. Exploring counterfactuals means exploring our own emotional reactions to the result of a counterfactual. 

Similarly, the consumption of fiction necessitates the construction of a mental micro-world. It allows us to simulate the thoughts, behaviors and actions of others within the ruleset of the make-believe. Similarly it allows us to entertain our own emotional reactions towards certain events within the model. 

Slide 4: The creation of a microworld. The simulationist model. 

It seems that both fiction and the imaginary worlds of children serve a similar purpose and have a similar structure. They both necessitate the construction of a mental model which contains relationships between things which allow for the exploration of counterfactuals and the exploration of our own emotional responses and attitudes. 

Research within psychology shows that we construct a mental model of the characters and events (Graesser et al., 2002). Furthermore, this type of mental model is similar to our entertaining of future or hypothetical events. This follows from psychological work on how we cognitively process fiction. 

For example, some psychologists take situation model construction to be an imaginative activity, allowing us to take the “mental leap into imagined worlds” (Zwaan, 1999 ). While this is not all that is done when one "imagines", it is a requirement in the exploration of any counterfactuals. Exploring what is possible and what is not. This type of simulationist model is the same one we employ in entertaining hypothetical or future events. 

"situation models are usually construed as having an analogical structure, for instance, representing relations between entities in an abstract spatial framework" (Tapiero, 2007) (Friend, 2021, pg. 29)

The use of these models is to inform our own behaviour. A core part of these models is the exploration of emotional responses, especially our own. In the exploration of counterfactuals emotions are a core part. (Graesser et al., 2002) Especially when exploring hypotheticals that concern ourselves. We are aware of the unreality of these things, yet they inspire genuine emotional responses because exploration of counterfactuals necessitates an emotional aspect. Things of this sort that allow us to explore our own behaviour also include the place of our own emotions. 

Slide 7: We participate in both the fictional and hypothetical models in a certain way. Because of the way we are "brought into" these worlds. The process of make-believe automatically inspires emotional reactions. Because we structure the story in the same way we structure the counterfactuals of our own life, which we have a possibility of entertaining. 

'‘It goes without saying that we are genuinely moved by novels and films and plays, that we respond to works of fiction with real emotion’ (Walton, 1997, p. 275).'

This helps in explaining our emotional reactions towards films, even though we are aware of their unreality. We cannot help but relate to them. We are taking part in the game of make-believe which structures our interaction in the same way we structure our own counterfactuals. Which necessitate emotions
