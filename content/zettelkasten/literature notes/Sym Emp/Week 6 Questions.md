Could the trouble that arises in our analysis of empathy be related to our treatment of it as an abstract and context-less object/emotion. That is, we believe it is a general emotion, in which its production in different circumstances produces the same thing. Similar to the way we view the arising of pleasure. In the Hollan reading, we are shown the ways in which empathy is response to a patterned set of stances and actions. Making it something which directly adjusts itself and relies on changing social contexts. Is our attempt to investigate empathy as a general emotion misguided? Should our discussion of it explicitly include the conditions that caused *this type* of empathy to arise? 

Furthermore, should we frame empathy as a genus of emotions that arise in relation to certain patterned actions. For example, different stances seem to produce different variants of empathy. In these cases, empathy is also closely related to action. Does empathy always need to be connected with action? Some would view our detached variant of empathy as not being empathetic at all. After all, if we are able to quasi-engage with the emotions of another and do nothing about it, this might seem cruel or immoral in a certain way.
