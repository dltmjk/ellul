1. Origin of the Term "quasi-emotion" 
	1. "Fearing Fictions" Kendall Walton 
		1. Emotional responses made specifically towards fictional events and entities are not genuine emotions 
		2. Because we have a lack of existential commitment to the objects of those emotions. Therefore, they have no motivational force 
			1. This is explicitly rejected in Colin Radford's "How can we be moved by the fate of Anna Karenina"
	2. There is no reason to take an existentially committed belief as a necessary condition for emotion. 
		1. "So the absence of an existentially committed belief about the intentional object of one’s affective response need not be taken to classify that response as non emotional." pg.43
		2. Emotion needs to be accompanied by a certain thought pg.43 
			1. Fear needs to be accompanied to a thought about danger 
			2. Therefore, beliefs are thought constituents of emotions 

What is missing in a fictional response? 
1. existential commitment to objects of one’s construal (a condition I have associated with both accounts, and one that has already been discussed);
	1. This has been argued against already. Emotions don't require existentially committed beliefs about their objects 
2. the endurance of the affective reaction beyond the moment during which one contemplates its object, which is thought less typical of responses to fiction (a condition espoused by Bouwsma; Walton does not concur, suggesting that responses to fiction may be prolonged);
3. the unalleviated discomfort typically associated with negative emotional responses, as opposed to the pleasure or satisfaction we can take in fictional tragedy or horror (once again, a condition put forward by Bouwsma; Walton disagrees, indicating that one may want to experience negative emotions in real-life contexts);
4. the kinds of behaviors, inclinations, motivations, and desires typically associated with emotional response to actual events
