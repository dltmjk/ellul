## Entities, constructions, and functions 
1. A *construction* of an arithmetic expression is like an itinerary. 
	1. You can go from LA to New York by travelling through St Louis, Chicago, and Boston
	2. However, there is no sense in which these are constituents of New York. 
		1. An itinerary is a compound in which a number of locations appear. 
2. Therefore, a calculation is very much like a compound in which several numbers and compounds interact to reach a result. We name this a *construction*
	1. A construction is linguistically made up of certain symbols, e.g $'2','3','-'$ which, together, create a *term*. e.g. $'2-3'$. The way these symbols are organized in this term is exactly parallel to the way they are organized in the construction.  
	2. A construction that has a unspecified number, e.g. (x-3), is named incomplete. 
3. While this construction is simply a string of symbols, the use of it comes from its application in representing a *calculating scheme* (or a function). Similar to the relationship between sheet music and the sound structure it produces. The construction and its resulting formula are isomorphic and should not be kept apart. 
	1. This was a redefinition of the function. 
		1. Previously - a particular method of getting from arguments to the corresponding values
		2. Redefined - *correspondence* between numbers which may be induced by such a method
	2. The method is extraneous to the function itself. The number one bears no trace of the particular way we used to derive it. 

## Two Views of Arithmetic 
1. View A
	1. The term is important in this case, not the number it leads to. 
		1. Something like $'(x.x)-3'$ is not an incomplete number name (like in view B) but a *complete name of an incomplete construction.*
		2. The 'gappy construction' of the term constitutes a definite function (mapping).
		3. He uses the term to discuss the incomplete construction, not the function
2. View B
	1. The term $'(2.2)-3'$ is simply a name for the number one. It names it through the process of a certain construction. 
		1. By using the term, it is not the construction that is important, but instead the term that it is naming, by *the means of* a certain construction. 
	2. An expression like $'(x.x)-3'$ is therefore an incomplete name for a number, so it names nothing at all. A number needs to fill this gap in order to make the term refer to something.  
		1. When a term is specified like this, the arithmetician is not talking about the incomplete construction, but instead of the corresponding function.

## The Linguistic Turn
1. While View A seems to be intuitively correct 
	1. E.g. being told that $(x+y).(x-y)$ is always equivalent to $(x^2-y^2)$ is more a statement about the congruence between the two constructions than about the proceeding results
	2. However, it has been radically rejected within modern semantic theory. 
		1. They might concede that the symbols each represent a mathematic entity, but the constructed term does not. 
		2. Therefore, they accept view B, an ontologically thrifty decision. 
			1. However, it pays a price in explanatory power 
				1. We cannot talk, in any meaningful way, about the construction itself. 
			2. Furthermore, if the term is only a certain way of naming a number, then the number must hold each construction within it. The term must be constitutive of the number. 
		3. There is a critique of [[Carnap Intentional Structure]]. 
			1. It is only valid for languages where the writing of arguments is done from left-to-right. 
		4. We also have some troublesome statements that don't have a corresponding number. For example, '$3 \div 0$', which is *undefined*. However, people who accept view B will have to concede that the term if empty. 
	3. To the pioneers of modern mathematics, a formula was a *diagram*. A graphical representation of a complex object. 
2. Quine tries to rehabilitate the sentence by stating that we should refrain from uttering sentences like  'Tom believes that 9-2=7' and say instead 'Tom believes-true "9-2=7"'.
	1. The problem with this is that the belief is not in the sentence. 
		1. An analogous example is that when we say a tide is counterclockwise, we are not saying it is relational to the direction of a clock. Instead it is relational to a certain direction which aligns with the clock. 
		2. Similarly, when we speak of a arithmetic statement, it is relational to some sort of entities, not as a mere syntax. 
		3. Therefore, tom believes what is *meant* by 9-2=7. Not in the sentence.
