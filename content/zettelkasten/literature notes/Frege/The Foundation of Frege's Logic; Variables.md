 ## Russell's Paradox
1. The conflation of constructions with what they construct is behind several of the counter-intuitive theses that Frege proposes. 
	1. This conflation is behind the eventual downfall of Frege's logical system
2. For a construction to be well-defined. Their formation *cannot* be circular. 
	1. Frege already erred when he defined the Functional value as consisting of the Function and the corresponding argument. 
		1. Since two is four divided by two, the number consists of the division function, four, and itself. 
3. The problem with defining a variable.
	1. "The range of a variable cannot contain anything that involves or presupposes that variable"

## The Vicious Circle Principle
1. It was Russell who discovered this flaw within Frege 
	1. Russell held the belief that propositions (objects of assertion) are compounds consisting of the objects they speak of. 
		1. They consist of terms. Terms are synonymous with *unit, individual, and entity*
	2. Both Russell and Frege wanted to know how we could make propositions about objects that are inaccessible to us. E.g. the solar system.
		1. Frege argues that we are only referencing an abstract representative, a *sense*
	3. Russell instead argues that the solar system is never our subject matter. Our real subject matter is certain attributes to which the point is related. 
2. Russell also has his conception of functions. In this case variables are not gaps but genuine constituents of the function. The function is a calculation scheme in which concrete objects are applied to obtain a concrete calculation. 
3. Russell gives his explanation of this vicious circle principle in the following passage 
	> "Whatever contains an apparent (i.e., bound) variable must not be a possible value of that variable... Thus whatever contains an apparent variable must be of a different type from the possible values of that variable; we will say that it is of a *higher type*. Thus the apparent variables contained in an expression are what determines its type" Bertrand Russell, Principles of Mathematics #quote 
	1. This helps us in resolving certain logic paradoxes and sets out Russell's theory of types.  
## An Objectual Notion of Variable 
1. However, Russell's theory does not give a satisfactory account of variables. 
	1. What is *not* the variable 
		1. Any particular individual
		2. A letter
	2. We need to answer how many distinct variables there are. If there is more than one, how do we define the difference? e.g. 'x is greater than y'
		1. Since he had no real satisfactory account of variables, he had no way of distinguishing between two variables. 
	3. Variables are given difference by appearing at specific instances within the sentence. 
	4. A variable is analogous to a point in space defined as ${x,y,z}$ coordinates. The x variable is the first coordinate in this sequence, the y is second and so on. Therefore, the ontological difference between variables is defined as the position in the sequence it returns.
