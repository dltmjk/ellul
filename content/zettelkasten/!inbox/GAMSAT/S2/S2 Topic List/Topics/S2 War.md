The true nature of war is buying and selling. Nations are compelled to war when their interests are trampled upon. War is a rational activity conducted by the ruling class. All appearances of irrationality are due to false justifications given for warlike behaviour. 

Point 1: All wars have material underpinnings, even if they are cloaked in the language of normative morality. 

Historical Example: The Union eventually framed the war as a moral crusade to end slavery (especially after the Emancipation Proclamation of 1863). Abolitionists argued it was a struggle for human freedom and the moral redemption of the United States

Slavery was not just a moral issue but the foundation of the Southern economy. Enslaved people represented immense capital wealth (by 1860, the monetary value of enslaved Africans exceeded all of America’s railroads and factories combined). Southern elites fought to preserve this economic system, which underpinned their agricultural exports (cotton, tobacco) that linked the Confederacy to global markets. 

The North’s resistance to slavery’s expansion westward was also materially motivated: free labor farmers and industrial capitalists feared the “slave power” would dominate new territories, skewing economic and political balance.

Contemporary Example: The 2003 US invasion of Iraq. Cloaked in the language of democracy and protection from supposed WMDs. 

Material Reality: Authors like Naomi Klein trace this. Iraq had the world’s second-largest proven oil reserves at the time. While the U.S. did not explicitly seize oil fields, scholars and critics argue that ensuring control over Middle Eastern oil supplies, stabilizing global energy markets, and protecting U.S. economic hegemony were underlying drivers. The post-war awarding of oil-related contracts to Western firms further reinforces this interpretation.
