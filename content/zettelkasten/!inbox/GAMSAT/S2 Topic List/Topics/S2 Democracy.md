French Revolution Political Background:

Bourgeoisie felt their class interests weren't represented on the political plane. Since they were excluded from political power on the basis they were not aristocratic born. 
This material imbalance of power formed a new ideology, around rationalism, freedom, and humanism. Centered around the bourgeois class. 
Representatives of the third estate demanded political representation. 
Their paradoxical demands for access to noble privileges and political representation helped to unleash dynamic revolutionary forces in France.
The rising social forces demanded a new superstructure. The expression this changing landscape took on was one that employed rationalist and humanist ideas following from Rousseau and Voltaire. 
The group was the bourgeoisie; its ideas were those of classic liberalism
One which, in theory, extended political participation to all, but in reality elevated the interests of the capitalist bourgeoisie. 

IMF Predatory Loans:

Ghana (1980s–90s): IMF structural adjustment imposed massive cuts to public spending, privatisation of state enterprises, and removal of subsidies. Democracy existed, but economic sovereignty was constrained.

Jamaica (1980s): IMF loans under Michael Manley’s government came with austerity measures that eroded the state’s ability to support the poor, while foreign creditors were prioritised. This led to severe social dislocation and undermined the democratic socialist project.

Argentina (1990s–2001): IMF-imposed neoliberal reforms tied the peso to the dollar, leading to eventual financial collapse. Governments elected by the people had little room to maneuver against debt obligations.
