Why are you applying for this specific programme, and how does it fit in with your future plans? * (1800 Characters) (250-300 Words)

My desire to pursue postgraduate study in philosophy is based on my belief that philosophical work is useful in itself. I believe that my areas of interest within philosophy, and the research projects I would like to pursue, match well with the faculty at KCL.

Ideas I have for certain research plans build on previous work I have completed. While working as a research assistant in early modern philosophy, I had the chance to explore how the Cavendish circle, through the ideas expressed in the “Short Tract” by Robert Payne, responded to the disagreements between Kenelm Digby, Thomas White, and Joseph Glanvill. This matches well with the research being conducted by Prof. Clare Carlisle. It also seems that White’s work, “The Institutions,” anticipates some major ideas in Spinoza’s Ethics. Spinoza, as a reader of Hobbes, could have encountered White’s ideas and built on them. I have presented on a similar topic at Zhengzhou University and would like to research this further.

Throughout my time in undergrad, I participated in a variety of reading groups. In one, we had to read “The Foundations of Frege’s Logic” by Pavel Tichý, which sparked an interest in both the philosophy and history of logic. Given my major interest in early modern philosophy, I started to explore how certain philosophers of the period (e.g. Galileo, Kepler) held anxieties over applying strict mathematical laws to the behaviour of physical objects. In continuing this research, I would like to see if any similar epistemological anxieties ever arose with the application of “formal” logic. This is similar to the work being done by Dr. John Callanan.

How does your experience and education make you a suitable candidate for this programme? (1800 Characters)

Although my interests are varied, I am most attracted to early modern philosophy, epistemology, and the philosophy of science. My honours thesis, which achieved a first‑class mark, explored questions in those last two areas by analysing problems of interpretability and structural modelling within Neural Networks. This type of research is similar to the type Dr. Alexander Franklin is conducting. My interest in the philosophy of science was developed by attending a number of postgraduate reading groups. Within one, I had the chance to lead a seminar on “Objectivity” by Daston and Galison, which allowed me to explore problems of epistemology in the philosophy of science.

As a research assistant, I have developed a grounding in a number of key figures, most notably Descartes, Hobbes, and Spinoza, as well as Kenelm Digby, Jan Baptist van Helmont, and the Cavendish Circle. One of my responsibilities as a research assistant includes finding relevant sources in both Latin and French, two languages in which I have reading proficiency. I have also audited Sanskrit classes taught by Prof. Geisshuesler and have some competency in reading Sanskrit texts.

In a philosophy of biology reading group, we explored theories that represent cognition as the Bayesian minimisation of a prediction error. It was through both of these groups, as well as an honours class on epistemic consequentialism, that I became interested in topics relating to formal epistemology, the philosophy and history of mathematics (particularly Frege), set theory, and Bayesian credences. I believe that, if given the opportunity, I would be a good addition to the university due to my diverse philosophical interests.
