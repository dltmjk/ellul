## Naming
Corn is a gigantic annual grass belonging to the family *Graminaeae*. It forms a part of the subtribe *Maydeae* and has 5 genera. Three American and two Oriental. Within scientific terms it is known as *Zea Mays*. Where *Zea* is either derived from the Greek which generally designates grains or cereals. 

*Mays* is probably from *Taino* which is the language spoken by aboriginal groups in the Antilles. *Mays* also means life giver. If both of these translations are accurate, the name for corn is redundant. 

## Growing 
The corn plant requires, on average, around 120 frost-free days with good sunlight in order to mature. The wide exposed surface area is suited for capturing more sun rays. If Corn is sewn away the equator, it is a summer crop. The lack of rain helps in germination, early development, and pollination. Corn does not require excess watering that is evenly distributed over its growth cycle. Dry-farmed corn does exist. 

Corn transforms light, heat, and other inorganic elements into biomass with *the least amount of waste*. It can be stored for long periods of time. 

## Varieties 
There are a lot of different corn varieties. Things that differ between these races 
1. Size: Ranging from 3 - 13 feet in height
2. Period between germination and pollination: Varies between 45 days to 150 days. 
3. Leaves: Anywhere from 8 - 48 
4. Ear Measurement: 4 - 24 inches
	1. Number of ears is always at least one but can be double or triple
5. Colors: 4 basic colors are white, yellow, red, purple/black. 
6. Commercial Varieties: flour corn, waxy maize, sweet corn, popcorn, flint, and dent
	1. Most corn races are either accidents of nature - natural crossings or free pollination
	2. There are over 250 corn races

## Structure 
A ear of corn usually has around 300 seeds aligned in rows. 500 is the average number of kernels on a ear, however, it can be as high as a thousand. These kernels are much larger than other cereals. The husk acts as a protective covering for the entire year. It shields the kernels from pests and moisture once they are mature. 

The root system of a corn plant requires relatively wide spacing between plants. They should be planted with adequate space and diagonal to each other. No-till corn is commonly employed. Allowing for sowing on steep hillsides. Corn is usually incorporated with squash and climbing beans. This is called the [[Three Sisters Method]]. This works because squash is a creeper that does not compete with corn for light. The shadow it casts (along with its foliage) restricts the growth of weeds and lowers the evaporation of ground moisture. The climbing bean is a legume that twines up the stalk of a corn plant. 

Pigweed, when found around corn plants, are tolerated because of their nitrogen fixing properties (which corn also has, unique among cereals). 

Corn has separate masculine and feminine flowers on the same plant. The masculine flowers on the ear or tassels that crown the stalk. Whereas, the feminine flowers on the silks adhering to the stalk. The separation of flowers made artificial pollination and hybrid seeds possible. 

## Usage
> Above all, in a good part of the world corn was the one enduring staple of peasant classes and societies. Corn represented a way of life and an organization of production that tolerated exploitation and dispossession well. Corn, however, never necessarily implied or required such burdens. Corn and Capitalism, Arturo Warman #quote

For Native Americans the stalks are used as wood replacement. You can also make juice from the stalks - akin to black honey. The leaf is used to feed horses and the *mihaui* (spike at the crown) is used to make bread. Nowadays, the stalk can be used to make paper. Corncobs are substituted for toilet paper. Cobs are used to hell the corn and to make pipes in the US. It can also be fermented and turned into alchohol.
