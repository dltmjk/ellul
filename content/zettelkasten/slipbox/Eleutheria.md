The Greek word for freedom, the equivalent within Latin is 'libertas' (albeit slightly more restricted). Represents freedom in terms of both the individual and the state. No equivalent exists within any other ancient language of the Near East or Asia. 

The medieval concept of the freeman is a person who "owing no obligation, not even deference, to an overlord is one of the most important if intangible legacies of medieval peasants to the modern world" Rodney Hilton, Bond Men Made Free: Medieval Peasant Movements and the English Rising of 1381 p. 235 #quote 

This type of relationship with the world can be traced back to the ancient Greeks. The liberation of the Greek peasantry from any sort of servitude to a lord or state produced a unique conception of freedom. At its heart, it represents a freedom from the *necessity* of having to work for another. Not freedom from labor but freedom of labor. Applied to both the individual and the state, for example a state that owes no tribute to another state.
