The meter was originally defined as one-hundred-thousandth of a quarter of the earth meridian. It was divided decimally, rather than by twelfths which was standard in pre-metric times. The proposal of the meter was first discussed by members of the Académie des sciences in Paris in 1790. While it was suggested both in 1793 and 1795. Historians usually attribute its launch to 1799 and 1800 at an event named "the first international conference". 

Despite its novelties as a symbol of the Napoleonic empire, the meter was banned at the Congress of Vienna in 1814-15. It was reintroduced in 1840 France by fueling the ambition of economic liberalism. It is from this second relaunch that we remember it.
