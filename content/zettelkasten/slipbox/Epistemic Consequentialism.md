A type of epistemology that draws an analogy between consequentialism in ethics (which holds an intrinsic good, e.g. pleasure) and *epistemic good*. In this view epistemic success should be based around aiming for maximizing the epistemic good. 

> Epistemic evaluation is undertaken from what we might call the “epistemic point of view.” That point of view is defined by the aim at maximizing truth and minimizing falsity in a large body of beliefs. William Alston, Concepts of Epistemic Justification p. 89 #quote 

Another characterization is given by Kristoffer Ahlstrom-Vij and Jeffrey Dunn

> (EC) Epistemic consequentialism is the idea that the epistemically right (e.g. the justied) is to be understood in terms of conduciveness to the epistemic good (e.g. true belief). Kristoffer Ahlstrom-Vij and Jeffrey Dunn, A Defense of Epistemic Consequentialism, pg 541
