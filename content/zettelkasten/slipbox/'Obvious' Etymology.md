Obvious, derived from the Latin *ob* and *viam*, meaning 'in the way'. Something being obvious means that it is so 'in the way' that you don't need to look for it. It will occur naturally and without trouble. 

Dubbed a "[[Contranym]]" by grammarians. By the 14th century, the stress on the prefix *ob* became far heavier and the word started to shift into emphasizing something akin to an unavoidable obstacle. Shifting from meaning something that is 'in the way' into something that has to be grappled with. 

This etymological backtracking account is given within [[The Making of Measure and the Promise of Sameness]]
