The European language cognates derive from the Latin adverbial or adjectival form *obiectivus/obiective*. Introduced by philosophers Duns Scotus and William of Ockham. The substantive/noun form does not emerge until the 19th century. 

The terms were originally swapped in meaning, "objective" referred to things that are presented to the conscious mind. For example, within Descartes *Meditations* he contrasted objective reality (things as they seem to our mind) with formal reality (things as they are in the external world). 

Eighteenth century dictionaries still preserved this usage: 
> Hence a thing is said to exist OBJECTIVELY, objective, when it exists no otherwise than in being known; or in being an Object of the Mind. Ephraim Chambers, “Objective/objectivus,” Cyclopaedia, or, An Universal Dictionary of Arts and Sciences (London: J. and J. Knapton, 1728), vol. 2, p. 649.

The two words fell into neglect during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries and were only invoked as technical terms by logicians and metaphysicians. Kant was the first to revive the terms within his own philosophy. However, his meanings were changed from the common usage. His 'objective' referred to the "forms of sensibility" (time, space, causality) that act as the preconditions of experience. His 'subjective' was used to refer to 'empirical sensations'. This distinction was concerned with universal and particular, not world and mind. 

However, it was Kant's usage that revived the terms employment. Despite the new meanings being somewhat confusing to readers (Coleridge writes that "has needlessly perplexed his reasoning by his strange use of Subjective and Objective — his Subjectivity = the Objectivity]of former Philosophers, and his Objectivity = their Subjectivity.) Samuel Taylor Coleridge, Biographia Literaria, or, Biographical Sketches of My Literary Life and Opinions

It was in the 1820s that the term gained its familiar feeling. First in German, than French, and finally in English. Objective - a relation to an external object. Subjective - a personal inner, inhering in us, in opposition to objective.
