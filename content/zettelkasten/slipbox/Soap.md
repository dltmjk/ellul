Soap acts a [[surfactant]] that emulsifies oils and allows it to be carried by water. Historically, soap was made through a combination of rainwater, animal fats/vegetable oil ([[Triglycerides]]), and ashes (Bases). When the [[Triglycerides]] is treated with a strong base, like $NaOH$ which results in salted fatty acids. This is essentially soap. 

![[Pasted image 20250427011128.png]]

One end has a strong charge separation, which makes it polar. Whereas the long carbon chain tail is non-polar. The former is hydrophilic and the latter is lipophilic. Non-polar molecules dissolve in non-polar liquids like oil, and polar molecules dissolve in polar liquids like water. Since this molecule has both, which is why soap is effective. 

With the fatty acids, the most important characteristic is the chain length. As chain length increases, it becomes less-polar and therefore less soluble within water. They are generally also harder and don't lather as easily. 

The base that is used will influence the fatty acid that is made in the end. 
1. Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) ones are generally harder and less water soluble. 
2. Potassium Hydroxide (KOH) are softer and more water soluble (Usually used to made liquid soaps)
3. Lithium Hydroxide (LiOH) is used for lubricants and grease
