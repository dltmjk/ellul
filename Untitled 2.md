  

As human beings we are faced with a myriad of ways to live our lives. Whether in service of other people or selfishly. These decisions require careful thought and philosophers have spoken at length on this topic. In this essay we will cover the viewpoints of three main worldviews. The Epicurans – resulting from the philosophy of Epicurus, Hedonists, and Ascetics.

  

A philosophy of moderation has taken many different forms. For example, Epicurus. An ancient Greek philosopher, known best for his theory that the universe consisted of indivisible “atoms”, asserted that pleasure was deeply connected with moderation. In his view, pleasure “wears itself out”. To maintain its status it requires bigger and more intense events. We can easily imagine a drug-addict seeking the next big high. While some critics may argue that this behaviour results from the way substances chemically interact with our bodies, Epicurus maintains that all pleasures follow this law. His solution is to live a life of moderation, with limited luxuries. Certain things are allowed but they must be used sparingly as to not lose their value. We will refer to people who practice this way of life as “Epicurans”.

  

Hedonists, those who argue that the goal of life should be sheer pleasure, argue against this. In their view all decisions can be re-evaluated as pleasure seeking. A person who acts self-sacrificially only does so because the pleasure gained from that behaviour outweighs the suffering incurred. Their argument is that humans should be pleasure seeking and all human decisions are actually pleasure seeking despite their appearance. They would argue that the Epicurans are actually acting in a way that is conducive to pleasure.

  

In rebutting this we draw from Nozick, an influential American philosopher. He gives a thought experiment. Let’s imagine that we one day invent a machine which mainlines dopamine straight into your brain, creating a high that will last for the rest of your life. Given the choice, would you use the machine, knowing that once started you will not want to leave. The hedonist feels this is an easy decision to make. Whereas Epicurus would dispute the way the question is formulated. He would argue that no such machine could exist and that the idea of infinite pleasure is contradictory. Pleasure requires something to contrast against, whether that is sadness, grief, or melancholy. This would result in them rejecting the machine. A decision not in line with the Hedonists view that all humans are pleasure seeking.

  

On the other side of the extreme are the Ascetics, who assert that the correct way to live life is in deprivation of all pleasures. They argue that this lifestyle allows for a connection with more spiritual pleasures. Something hidden from both the Epicurans and the Hedonists. In connecting with these spiritual pleasures, life gains a type of fulfilment only accessible when one is denied the material world.

While this is prima facie convincing, it is unclear why these spiritual pleasures are denied to those who partake in the material world. While most religious sages and monks are those estranged from society. It seems possible to have a connection with the spiritual without having to commit yourself to this extreme lifestyle. The Epicurans would argue that this sort of spiritual fulfilment is still possible if practised occasionally.

  

In conclusion, we have covered the philosophy of the Epicurans, hedonists, and ascetics. Reviewing each argument for their way of life, we conclude that the Epicurans provide the best points for their way of life.
